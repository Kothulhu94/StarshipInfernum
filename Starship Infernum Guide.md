### Chapter 1: Creating Characters

All of the players (except for the Dealer) will narrate the actions and effects of a single character in the story 2\.  
These characters are usually competent career astronauts, brave explorers, or interstellar service personnel that have been swept into a life or death emergency aboard a large space faring vessel 2\.  
The Dealer does not have only one specific character to manage 2\. Instead, they control the circumstances surrounding the crises on the ship as well as the obstacles, adversaries, and other characters that the PCs encounter as they try to survive and resolve the mounting crises 2\.  
Players can either create a unique character from scratch or may choose to use one of the eight sample characters provided at the end of this book 2\.  
Creating a new character is relatively easy and should take between five and ten minutes, as there are only a few choices that need to be made in order to play the game 2\.

#### What makes a good PC?

Think about the characters in your favorite sci-fi movies or books 2\. Usually the protagonists are heroes with almost human capabilities but possess significant personal flaws 2\. Ultimately you will want to make a character that you can have fun playing in a story about high-stress situations 2\.

* Maybe they are the captain of a vessel, seemingly in control but unable to save the entire crew 2\.  
* Maybe they are a brilliant mechanic, determined to keep the ship running by any means possible 2\.  
* Maybe they are a quirky scientist, whose curiosity has gotten out of hand 2\.  
* Maybe they are a logical android, determined to follow their primary function of helping the crew even if it means sacrificing themselves 2\.  
* Maybe they are a weary wage slave, putting their life on the line for some distant corporation and just hoping to make it home alive 2\.

Whoever they are, the characters in *Starship Infernum* wind up in a terrible situation, running out of options, and destined to meet their end 3\. Space is a cold and unforgiving place no matter how much you scream 3\.  
The Dealer will decide (usually with input from the rest of the players) roughly what sort of starship the characters are on, what the circumstances leading up to the present moment were, and what major crisis has now unfolded 3\.  
Sample scenarios are provided in Chapter 5 as a starting point, and these may place restrictions on the types of characters the players should create 3\.  
If the group prefers, the characters do not all need to follow the same theme, but it should make sense that they are all somehow on the same ship together 3\.  
After a scenario has been chosen, it is up to the individual players to determine who their character is and what they can do by defining a Concept, Traits, and an Aptitude 3\. These details are noted on the character sheet 3\.  
The Dealer always has the final say on what makes sense for a character and if a particular Concept, Trait, or Aptitude works for the current game 3\.

#### Step 1: Concept

Every PC needs a short two or three word Concept that roughly defines who their character is 3\. This usually takes the form of a descriptive generalization 3\.  
Examples include: *Heroic Captain, Logical First Mate, Plucky Cadet, Grumpy Engineer, Bloodthirsty Warrior, Empathetic Counselor, Depressed Android, Matter of Fact Medic,* etc 3\.  
Each player should also come up with a name for their character 3\. This is usually a straightforward task, though many players skip this step and come back to it as the final finishing touch 3\.  
Players should determine their character's approximate age, apparent gender, and general appearance to help paint a mental picture of their character and enable quality role-playing 3\.

#### Step 2: Traits

Next, each player must define three Traits that dictate special intrinsic qualities that their character can use to survive 3\.  
These Traits should be a few words at most and can be whatever makes sense to the player 3\. The Dealer should help define Traits as needed 3\.  
Examples include: *Cool Under Pressure, Expert Marksman, Small Enough to Hide Behind Anything, Unflappable Optimism, Logically the Odds are in Our Favor, I Can Handle a Bit of Blood,* etc 4\.  
As the three Traits are defined, the player should assign them a value as follows 4:

* One Trait is noted as \+/-3 4  
* A second Trait with \+/-2 4  
* The final Trait with \+/-1 4

#### Step 3: Aptitude

Every character in *Starship Infernum* has a special Aptitude which reflects a specific ability or the focused training that allows them to perform their high-stress job in the dangerous depths of outer space 4\.  
For most characters this will be directly related to their function on the ship, but for others (such as aliens) it may be a matter of their biological form or alternative psychology 4\.  
Players are free to choose any of the noted Aptitudes below, but it is usually best for a crew to diversify in their individual abilities 4\.  
If the Dealer allows, the players can come up with their own unique Aptitudes as well 4\.  
All Aptitudes should be approved by the Dealer to ensure they fit the scope of the story being told 4\.

#### Aptitude Options

**Android**You are unaffected by the Extreme Temperature, Lack of Oxygen, and Toxic Gas obstacles and may perform an EVA without a spacesuit 4\.  
**Armored**Due to military equipment or your unique biology, you may ignore one round of Rising Tension in all tests versus Adversaries 4\.  
**Commander**You know how to look out for and manage your team 4\. You may swap the last card you were dealt with the last card dealt to any other player in a Group Test 4\. This may allow you to retroactively bust 4\. Usable once per test 4\.  
**Counselor**You have insight to the way the mind works 4\. You may ignore one round of Rising Tension in tests that attack your psyche (*Panic, Claustrophobia, Paranoia,* etc) 4\.  
**Engineer**You are a mechanical wizard 4\. If you fail to repair a system when resolving a Crisis, you may take an immediate second attempt 4\.  
**Medic**Life is in your hands 5\. You may heal a Busted Trait on yourself or another character with a medkit obtained from the Med Bay, Escape Pods, or Science Lab 5\. Medkits are one use items and may only be used after the immediate obstacle in a room has been eliminated 5\.  
**Militant**You know the value of a good weapon at your side 5\. You start with your choice of a ranged or melee weapon, or explosives 5\. In addition, if you find them on the ship, you may continue to hold one of each 5\.  
**Psychic**You can read minds 5\. The Dealer plays with both cards face up in all solo tests against this character 5\. This applies to the first hand of the test only 5\.  
**Sanitation**You know your way around the ship 5\. You may flip two room cards before entering and choose which one it will be 5\. The other card becomes the obstacle for that room 5\. Remember that higher numbers (and the Ace of Spades) generally trigger harder obstacles 5\.  
**Security**Through strength and tactics, you can gain the upper hand against any Adversary 5\. You always win ties in tests versus Adversaries 5\.  
**Shapeshifter**You are not always what you seem to be 5\. You may swap the last card dealt to you with the Dealer's face-up card, once per test 5\.  
**Science**Careful attention to detail along with measurable and repeatable results are the mainstays of your profession 5\. Once per test, you may redraw the last card dealt to you in a bust on a non-Adversary Survival Test 5\.  
**Smuggler**The odds are always in your favor, because you know how to cheat 5\. You are dealt a single card from the Survival Deck at the start of the game 5\. You may swap this card with the last one dealt to you, once per test 5\. The new card remains with you for future use 5\.  
**Survivor**You are adept at staying out of harm's way 5\. If you survive the first round of a Disaster, you may slink away and hide thus ignoring any further rounds of that test 5\.  
**Trainee**You are going to screw up, and everyone knows it 5\. In Group Tests where you are present, the first player to bust (including yourself) may curse your name and immediately draw a new card to replace their last one 5\. Usable once per test 5\.

### Chapter 2: Playing the Game

The Dealer begins the game by narrating the current situation (including the Major and Minor Crises), a description of the starting room, and what the characters encounter there.  
The players will then take turns describing who their characters are and what they are doing.  
As the players explore the ship, the Dealer will draw two cards from the Room & Obstacle Deck and compare these cards, in any order, to the Room & Obstacle chart on page 50\.  
The Dealer will describe what the characters encounter in each room and the other players respond by describing what their characters say and do.  
The Dealer describes what happens next and the players respond, back and forth, so on and so on, until a test is required.  
**•Basic Setup•**

1. Shuffle both decks of cards individually. Do not shuffle them together. Decide which deck is the **Survival Deck** and which one is the **Room & Obstacle Deck**. Place these decks within easy reach of the Dealer.  
2. Place a number of counters equal to the number of the players, minus the Dealer, in the center of the table to form the **Crisis Clock**.  
3. Remove a single Ace *(any Ace is fine)* and one other random card from the Survival Deck. Place both removed cards face up next to each other in the center of the table. The Ace represents the **Major Crisis**, the other card denotes a **Minor Crisis**.  
4. Count out a number of **Jokers** equal to the number of players, minus the Dealer.  
* Shuffle 1 Joker face up into the Survival Deck.  
* Place 1 Joker on the Minor Crisis card you drew.  
* Place the rest of the Jokers on the Major Crisis card *(the Ace)*.

Not all rooms must have a life threatening obstacle, and clever players may be able to avoid any obstacle by narration alone.  
Sometimes a player may want to attempt something where the outcome is uncertain, but death is not a likely result of failure, such as hiding in a cabinet, climbing a broken ladder, or barricading a door. This is resolved by a **Simple Test** *(see page 20\)*.  
Anytime a player wants their character to do something inherently dangerous though, such as trying to avoid or escape from an explosion, fight or flee from an adversary, or face their own psychological breakdowns, they will have to check to see if they succeed or if they take damage in the attempt. This is called a **Survival Test** *(see opposite page)*.  
All tests are resolved through one or more hands of Blackjack against the Dealer.

#### The Basics of Blackjack

In *Starship Infernum*, the core mechanics are based on the classic game of Blackjack, but our rules go beyond the simple basics by employing Traits, Aptitudes, Group Tests, Natural 21s, Interrupts, and Rising Tension.  
First though, it helps if everyone knows how to play Blackjack in its most common form.  
**The goal of Blackjack is to get the highest total value on your cards without going over 21\.**

* Numbered cards are worth their numbered value.  
* Face cards *(King, Queen, and Jack)* are always worth 10\.  
* Aces are worth either 1 or 11, and can change value as needed.

Players are each dealt one card face up and the Dealer gets one card face down.  
A second face-up card is dealt to all involved players and one face up to the Dealer.  
Starting with the player to the Dealer's left, that player may choose to **hit** *(take another card)* or **stand** *(keep their total)*.  
Repeat this process until that player goes over 21 *(a bust)* or chooses to stand. Play then passes clockwise to the next player.

* **Once all the players have chosen to stand (or busted), the Dealer reveals their face-down card and attempts to get the highest total value, without busting, by taking extra cards.**  
* **When the Dealer stands, those players who have a higher total than the Dealer win. Those who have busted, or end with a lower total than the Dealer, lose.**

#### Beyond the Basics

**Using TraitsExhausting Traits**In any test, a player may adjust their final score up or down by the value of a single relevant Trait from their character sheet *(+/- 3, \+/- 2, or \+/- 1\)*.  
However, regardless of if they ultimately succeed or fail on the test, they must mark that the Trait value used has been exhausted, and they may not use it again until that Trait has been recovered.  
Once a player stands, they may not adjust the card values with a Trait *(i.e. no changing your mind once you see what cards the Dealer has)*.  
**Recovering Traits**Any time a player achieves a Natural 21 *(an Ace and a card worth 10\)* they may recover an Exhausted Trait. They are also able to recover an exhausted trait by using a medkit or by resting for a full turn in a room the Dealer has declared as a Safety. These rooms are specifically indicated on the Room & Obstacle chart.  
**Performing TestsSurvival Tests**Anytime a character faces off against an obstacle or attempts something inherently dangerous and risky, they must undergo a Survival Test.  
Once a Survival Test has begun, each character involved must declare how they will attempt to overcome the obstacle they are facing. This can be anything that makes sense, such as shooting an adversary with a laser gun, diving away from an explosion, or biting their tongue and pushing through a psychological breakdown.  
**•Things to Remember•**

* Players are always trying to beat the Dealer, never each other.  
* Players' cards are always dealt face up. Everyone can see them.  
* The Dealer's first card is always dealt face down.  
* A bust always inflicts damage.  
* A Natural 21 always ends the current round of a test and allows the player who drew it to recover an Exhausted Trait.  
* Anything that alters or effects a Player's cards must be done **before** the Dealer shows their face-down card. After that, values cannot be changed until the hand is finished.

Players do not need to make use of their character's Traits in order to participate in a Survival Test, but they will be able to exhaust Traits relevant to their action in order to adjust their final score.  
The Dealer begins by dealing a single face-up card to each player in the test, and one face down card to themselves.  
A second face-up card is dealt to each player and a final face-up card to themselves. Unless Rising Tension is in effect *(see opposite)* each player *(including the Dealer)* should now have two cards.

* **If the Dealer has a Natural 21** *(an Ace and a card worth 10\)* and the player(s) do not, the first round of the test is automatically over.

New cards are dealt to everyone involved, but the players begin this new hand with one level of Rising Tension.

* **If a player has a Natural 21** and the Dealer does not, the test is immediately over and the players have come out on top.  
* **If nobody has a Natural 21,** the first player in the test *(starting clockwise from the Dealer's left)* must decide whether to keep the value showing on their cards *(stand)* or to take another card to add to the total *(hit)*.

After standing, a player may choose to add or subtract the value of a relevant Trait to their final total.  
Repeat this process until the player goes over 21 *(a bust)* or decides to stand and stop taking cards. A bust may be mitigated by subtracting a Trait even after the total shows more than 21\.

* **If a player busts,** they have failed horribly and incur a significant loss. This is represented by crossing any Trait *(exhausted or not)* and its value off of their character sheet.

This loss is permanent and cannot be recovered *(except by the Medic Aptitude)*. The Dealer narrates what happened to the character that causes this damage.

* **If all players bust,** the test is over and the Dealer does not need to continue.

**Once all players stand,** the Dealer flips over their face-down card.

* **If the Dealer has beaten** a player's score, the Dealer wins the test. If not, they must continue to draw cards until they have beaten or tied the player's total. *(If multiple players are involved in a test, see **Group Tests** on page 20).*  
* **If the Dealer busts** *(more than 21\)*, the player(s) win the test, but nothing bad happens to the Dealer *(or the ship)*.  
* **If the Dealer wins,** they narrate the ongoing struggle and a new, albeit harder test begins with the same players(s) *(see Rising Tension below)*.  
* **If a player wins,** that player narrates their character's victory.  
* **If a player and Dealer tie** *(a push)*, the struggle continues on with neither side having succeeded or failed.

In a push, the current cards are discarded, new cards are dealt, and the hand is played again without triggering further Rising Tension *(see below)*.  
**Rising Tension**Once a Survival Test has begun, the focus is squarely on the character(s) caught in the test. If they fail a hand, that failure should be narrated in order to ramp up the tension and keep the game exciting.  
But the character(s) will continue to struggle, without reprieve, until they either succeed by winning the test or they bust and therefore lose a Trait as damage. Only then does the focus move on to the rest of the characters.  
In most Survival Tests, whenever a player fails a hand, a new hand is dealt to both the losing player and the Dealer over and over again, until the player eventually busts or succeeds.  
However, each successive hand of the test becomes considerably harder. This increase in difficulty is known as Rising Tension.

* **If a players fails the first hand** and moves on to a new one, they automatically receive three new starting cards instead of the normal two.

They still may choose to hit and stand as they wish, unless they have already achieved a bust with their three card hand. They are also still able to use an unexhausted trait if they wish, to modify their final score.  
Each failed hand ramps up the tension even more and adds another card to the next draw.  
A player that fails twice will begin the third hand of a test with four cards, the fourth hand with five cards, and so on until they beat the Dealer or they finally bust.  
**Simple Tests**Simple tests are used when the challenge at hand is not a life or death situation.  
Perhaps a character wants to hide before an Adversary enters the room or is going to try to climb back up a trapdoor chute. Maybe they need to barricade a door or they want to hack into a computer terminal to shut down the automatic defenses.  
Cards for a Simple Test are dealt from the Room & Obstacle Deck, instead of the Survival Deck. Unexhausted Traits may still be used to modify the total.

* **A success on the test** means that the character manages to do whatever it is that they were trying to do.  
* **A failure on a Simple Test** just means that the character does not succeed and might be able to try again the next round, if there are no pressing obstacles present.  
* **A bust on the Simple Test** means that the character has failed horribly and must lose a Trait as damage. They are normally not able to attempt the same Simple Test again.

**Simple Tests do NOT trigger the Rising Tension mechanic.**  
**Group Tests**Occasionally a test may affect all the characters in a room *(i.e. poison gas, an explosion, or an electrified floor)*. In this case, every affected character participates in the test using their own cards against the Dealer.  
The Dealer still only has one set of cards and compares results with each player individually. It is possible in Group Tests for some players to succeed while others fail.  
In a Group Test however, the Dealer has certain limits placed on their hand. The Dealer must hit if their total is 16 or less and must stand if it is 17 or above.  
In determining whether to hit or stand, an Ace is always counted as an 11, unless that would make the Dealer bust. When determining if the Dealer wins or busts, the Ace's value is whatever is most beneficial to the Dealer's hand.  
*Example: A Dealer has an Ace and a 5 as their initial hand, for a total value of 16\. They must hit and take another card.*  
*The Dealer draws a King, which would make the current total either 26 or 16\. The most beneficial value is a 16, so again the Dealer must take a hit and draw another card.*  
*They draw a 4, bringing their final total to 20\. They may NOT draw another card, as their hand value is now over 17\.*  
**Note:** If any player in the Group Test is dealt a Natural 21, and the Dealer does not have a Natural 21, then all players have won the Group Test and no further checks are needed.

#### Damage and Death

Anytime a player busts in a hand, no matter the cause or nature of the test, their character must take damage.  
Damage is represented by crossing out an entire Trait on the player's character sheet. Busted traits are not recoverable, except through the Medic Aptitude.

* **Once a player loses their last trait from a bust, they are dead.** Deceased crewmates are still part of the game, however, and linger on as memories in the minds of the remaining characters.

Dead PCs keep one card of their choice from the hand that killed them *(theirs or the Dealer's)*.  
In the rare case that a character dies on a Simple Test, they still pick a card but take that card from the Survival Deck instead.

* **The Dead PC may swap their held card for the last card dealt to another PC, before the Dealer's cards are revealed, once per test.**

When they do this, the Dead PC must narrate a memory of an interaction that they had with the character relevant to the current situation. It is their choice whether to help or hinder the PCs.  
The Dead PC keeps the new card they received in the trade and can then swap this card out in future tests.  
If a living character encounters a Safety room *(determined by the Room & Obstacle Deck)* after any crewmates have died, the living character must beat a Simple Test with one level of automatic Rising Tension in order to take advantage of the Safety and recover an Exhausted Trait.

#### Interrupting

It is possible for a player not originally engaged in a test to declare an interrupt and join in, but only if the original player has a matching pair *(a split)* for their first two cards.  
A split is only possible when two cards actually match, not simply when they have the same value.  
*Example: Two Aces could be split, but a 10 and Queen could not.*  
Interrupting may only be done on the first hand of a test, never on Rising Tension.  
**Note:** If someone insists on interrupting against the original player's wishes *(such as when they have a pair of Kings)*, the rules allow them to do so.  
However, such behavior is usually a very bad idea and is not really conducive to a cooperative gaming experience.  
**When a player interrupts,** the matching pair is split between the two players, and a 2nd card is dealt to the Interrupting Player before the Dealer's second card is drawn.  
Focus then shifts over to the Interrupting Player alone for a single hand *(no Rising Tension)* to determine if they successfully draw attention away from the original character.

* **If the Interrupting Player succeeds on their test,** they draw the focus and defeat the obstacle.  
* **If the Interrupting Player fails,** then they are out of the test and narrative focus returns to the original player.

The Dealer then draws a new face-down card before dealing a new face-up card to both the original player and themselves.

* **If the Interrupting Player busts,** they are damaged by the obstacle and lose a Trait, but have managed to pull focus and give the others a chance to flee. The original player does not need to complete the Survival Test.

#### Resolving Crises

When the players enter a room designated by their particular crisis, or when cards of the same suit or number *(a match)* are drawn when setting up a new room and obstacle, the players may attempt a test to resolve a step of a crisis.  
This Crisis Test is resolved via a Simple Test AFTER the obstacle in the room has been defeated.

* **If the player succeeds on the Crisis Test,** remove a Joker on the relevant Crisis Card from the game. They may not complete more than one step in a single room and must move on to progress.  
* **If the player fails the Crisis Test,** a Joker is not removed, and a NEW obstacle card is immediately drawn and encountered.

After the new obstacle is defeated, another attempt may be made to resolve a step of the crisis. This may be done up to three times per room.

* **If the player busts on the Crisis Test,** a Joker is removed from the Crisis Card and shuffled into the undrawn cards of the Survival Deck. No more Crisis Tests may be attempted in this room.  
* **Once all of the Jokers from a crisis have been resolved,** a final test must be made to resolve the Crisis Card itself *(in a different room from the previous tests)*.  
* **Once a Crisis Test is won for the Crisis Card itself,** that threat has been eliminated, and all Jokers *(from any Crisis)* are removed from the Survival Deck. The original Crisis Card returns to the Survival Deck, and the deck is reshuffled.

**Note:** Crisis Tests inside the ship are always non-fatal, one-round Simple Tests with no Rising Tension.  
Some Crises may involve fighting aliens, repelling invaders, or squashing mutinies. Even though these situations would normally use the Survival Deck, when the players are resolving a Crisis Test they should use the Room & Obstacle Deck instead.  
When players fail such a test, they simply do not gain the edge against the enemy, though damage is still taken by the character in the case of a bust.

#### Extravehicular Activities (EVA)

There may be systems that can only be repaired via exiting the ship and working on the outside of the hull. This is a slow and dangerous process which can result in a crewmate falling off the ship and being lost to space.  
EVAs always require a space suit and can only be initiated from an Airlock, Hangar Bay, or a room that has the Hull Breach obstacle.  
EVAs do not draw obstacles or trigger normal Survival Tests.

* **To travel along the outside of the ship,** characters in an EVA must attempt a Simple Test using the Room & Obstacle Deck.

Whether this test succeeds or not, the EVA crew member makes note of their progress. This is best done by using small counters, chits or tokens, or by making a mark on the character sheet.

* **If the test fails,** they make no progress but may try again the next round with no Rising Tension.  
* **If they bust on the test to travel,** they take damage.  
* **Once an EVA crew member has succeeded on the Simple Test to travel,** they may attempt a Survival Test to repair a damaged external system *(and thus resolve a step of a Crisis)* on their next turn.

EVA characters use the Survival Deck instead of the Room & Obstacle Deck when repairing a system, to reflect the dangers of working outside the starship.

* **If the EVA character succeeds on the test,** remove a Joker or Crisis Card as normal.  
* **If the player fails the test, they MAY choose to stop at any point** and spend their next turn traveling along the ship to a new spot or they may continue the current Survival Test with Rising Tension.  
* **If they choose to stop and move to a new spot,** they must attempt Simple Tests to travel again, gaining a counter for each round they travel.

Once they pass a new Simple Test, they have reached a spot where they can work on the ship again and may try another Survival Test to repair a system.

* **For every three travel counters an EVA crew member accrues working outside the ship,** they must face a Survival Test that represents the inherently risky nature of their mission.

This test is resolved as normal, using the Survival Deck, and is subject to Rising Tension. This Survival Test does not help resolve a Crisis however.

* **At any point, the EVA crew member may attempt to return to the ship.** Returning to the ship requires beating a Simple Test to remove a counter.

For every three counters removed, the EVA crew member must face a Survival Test as above. Once all counters are gone, the character successfully returns to the ship.  
EVA crew MUST participate in any Disasters or Survival Tests drawn by players inside the ship that could affect the outside of the ship, such as the Hull Breach and External Impact obstacles.

#### Weapons & Gear

**Spacesuits**Spacesuits are required to perform an EVA *(except for those with the Android Aptitude)*. While worn they make the wearer immune to Contamination, Lack of Oxygen, Toxic Gas, and Radiation obstacles. However, wearing a spacesuit prevents a character from using their Traits to modify any test, inside or outside the starship.  
Spacesuits can usually be found in Airlocks, Escape Pods, and the Hangar Bay rooms. Characters wearing a spacesuit may not carry any other gear.  
**MedKits**MedKits can be found in the Medical Bay, Storage Rooms, and Escape Pods, or assembled from supplies in the Science Labs with a Simple Test.  
These are basic first aid kits that may be used one time each to recover an Exhausted Trait in any room, if there are no immediate obstacles to face. Characters with the Medic Aptitude may use them to heal a busted Trait instead of recovering an exhausted one.  
**Weapons**Weapons can be found in the Security HQ and Armory on a starship. Melee weapons allow a player to redraw the last card dealt to them in a Survival Test versus Adversaries in close range, once per test.  
Ranged weapons allow a player to redraw the last card dealt to them versus Adversaries at a distance. If you bust on a test in which you used a weapon, that weapon is destroyed.  
**Explosives**Explosives can be found in the Science Lab or Storage Rooms. A successful Survival Test is required to prepare the explosives for use. Once prepped they can be used to blow a door open, create a hull breach, or completely eliminate an Adversary of any level.  
**Communication**In most circumstances, shipboard PC Crewmates may use wireless communications built into their uniforms or spacesuits to talk with each other from various parts of the ship.  
**Note:** Individual characters may only carry one piece of gear at a time. *(The Militant Aptitude may carry one of each type.)*

### Chapter 3: Running the Game

In most contemporary role-playing games, the job of the person running the game is to keep the story flowing and ensure that all the players are having a good time. This should absolutely be the first priority of the Dealer in *Starship Infernum* as well. Unlike most other RPGs though, the Dealer's second priority is to KILL THE CHARACTERS\!  
The rules in *Starship Infernum* are mostly straightforward, and there is no real room for pulling punches to favor the efforts of the players. If they fail in a Survival Test, then the tension rises and things should get worse for them.  
If the player busts on a test, then things get really bad\! They have lost a Trait and are one step closer to death. There is no way around that.  
The Dealer is also the one that helps keep the tension up and makes things suspenseful. They need to be able to improvise descriptions, think on their feet, roll with what the cards give them, and have a good grasp of pacing.  
The Dealer must resist the urge to stonewall the other players or limit their options. The deck is already stacked in the Dealer's favor; that is just the way the game of Blackjack works.  
If a player wants to do something original in an attempt to overcome an obstacle, the Dealer should find ways to say "Yes" to the attempt. Let the cards determine if the player is successful or not.  
Lastly, the Dealer should try to encourage everyone to get in on the action. Take the players' suggestions on how rooms and obstacles manifest. Listen to the players to figure out what makes them feel the pressure, but also allow them revel in the moments that they get to be the heroes. Try to keep the players' attentions focused on survival and lead them away from attacking each other.  
Encourage the Dead PCs to be imaginative with their flashbacks and remind them that they are still characters in the story too, and not just a cool game mechanic.  
In the end, these stories are meant to be exciting and enjoyable. Even when the entire team dies, they should still have had a good time playing the game.

#### Disasters

The core conflict at the heart of *Starship Infernum* is the Crises, and these manifest during the game as Disasters.  
The game begins with one Joker already in the Survival Deck and several Jokers on the table atop their respective Crisis Cards.

* **When a Joker is drawn from the Survival Deck,** it triggers a crisis-specific Disaster immediately after the current obstacle is resolved.

If multiple Jokers are drawn during a challenge, the PCs only have to face off against a single Disaster.  
The Disaster is resolved as a Group Test against ALL surviving crew members using the Room & Obstacle Deck instead of the Survival Deck. *(This is so that the Crisis Clock does not advance unfairly during a long Disaster test.)*  
**Note:** Rising Tension is applied differently during a Disaster test.  
A Disaster Survival Test will continue until everyone beats the Disaster in the same hand, or at least one player busts and takes damage.

* **If a player is dealt a Natural 21,** the Dealer is beaten and the Disaster is immediately averted.  
* **If a player busts,** they take damage and the Disaster ends. No one else needs to test.  
* **If every player beats the Dealer in the same Round,** then the Disaster is averted.  
* **If at least one player fails the round, but no players bust,** deal a new hand to everyone. Players that beat the Dealer receive new cards but no Rising Tension.

Players that failed the test receive new cards with one extra card of Rising Tension.  
Continue round after round with increasing Rising Tension until someone busts and takes damage, or everyone manages to beat the Dealer in the same round.  
**Beating a Disaster does NOT resolve a phase of the Crisis** *(i.e. do not remove a Joker)*.  
Once the Disaster has been resolved, set the Joker(s) that triggered it aside. These cards will be returned to the Survival Deck when that deck is reshuffled at the end of a Reel.

#### Reshuffling

The span between one shuffle of the Survival Deck and the next is known as a **Reel** *(as in a movie reel)*.  
A game of *Starship Infernum* usually lasts for a number of Reels equal to the number of Player Characters plus one. However, if you are playing with only two PCs it is better to play for a full four Reels to give them a solid chance.

* **When you run out of cards in either deck, reshuffle that deck and keep playing.** Reshuffling the Room & Obstacle Deck is no big deal, but a reshuffle of the Survival Deck entails special rules and must be tracked *(see The Crisis Clock rules below)*.  
* **When you reach the end of the Survival Deck and must reshuffle it,** make sure you put any Joker that was drawn from the deck back into it. Put the Jokers in face up, so that they are easy to spot.

#### The Crisis Clock

Every game of *Starship Infernum* has at least two Crises that must be resolved within a limited amount of time.  
A pile of tokens in the center of the table *(the Crisis Clock)* is used to keep track of how much time is remaining. The pile should start with a number of tokens equal to the number of players *(or three if there are only two players and a Dealer)*.

* **Each time the Survival Deck is reshuffled, remove one token from the Crisis Clock.**  
* **If you reach the end of the Survival Deck and cannot remove another token,** time has run out and the game ends.

#### Major Crisis

Every session of *Starship Infernum* should have one Major Crisis *(represented by an Ace card)* that the players must fully resolve to win the game.  
Dealers should choose a crisis from the list below, or use one assigned by a specific scenario later in the book.

##### •Major Crises•

* **Alien Horror** ............. a predatory alien stalks the ship killing off the crew  
* **Collision Course** ......... the ship is about to crash into something deadly  
* **Contaminated** .................... the entire crew is sick, and it is getting worse  
* **Dead Ship** .................. adrift and disabled, the ship isn't going anywhere  
* **Dimensional Rift** ............... the fabric of reality has been ripped asunder  
* **Invasion** ........ outside forces have assailed the ship and begun boarding  
* **Mutiny** ........ the captain has been overthrown, and now mob rule reigns

**Alien Horror**There is a horrible alien presence lurking inside the ship that will attempt to devour the entire crew.Crisis Tests to attack the Alien via traps or automated systems can be attempted on matches of the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the following locations:

* **Hangar Bay:** Open a path to the vacuum of space.  
* **Engineering:** Shut down the engines so that the ship does not explode.  
* **Life Support:** Change the oxygen mix in an area of the ship to ignite.

**Disaster:**The Alien Horror ambushes the group. Only the crew members who were in the room where the Joker was drawn must participate in this Disaster.  
**Collision Course**Your starship is on a crash course with something that will kill you all. This could be a black hole, a star, an uninhabitable planet, or a massive asteroid.Crisis Tests to bring critical systems back online can be attempted on matches of the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the following locations:

* **Navigation:** Repair/reprogram the navigation system.  
* **EVA:** Get the emergency thrusters back online.  
* **Bridge:** Pilot away from the target before it is too late.

**Disaster:**Turbulence rocks through the ship threatening to collapse hull sections or possibly even create a breach that could suck PCs out into space.  
**Contaminated**The inhabitants of the ship have been exposed to an extinction-level disease, but they might not all be infected yet.The crew has two options to resolve this crisis, noted below, or may attempt a Crisis Test on matches of the Room & Obstacle Deck.  
**Note:** If a player takes damage in a Disaster, then they are now infected and cannot be saved with Option 2\.  
**Option 1: Cure Everyone**

* **Med Clinic:** Isolate the disease in an infected person.  
* **Science Lab:** Synthesize a cure.  
* **Life Support:** Distribute the cure throughout the ship.

**Option 2: Escape with a Cure**

* **Med Clinic:** Clear the PC crew members as uninfected.  
* **Engineering:** Set the ship to explode.  
* **Escape Pods:** Escape the ship, leaving the diseased behind.

**Disaster:**Symptoms of the illness develop in the PCs or their existing symptoms worsen.  
**Dead Ship**The ship has totally lost power and all systems, including life support and communication, have begun shutting down. The ship might have crashed onto a lifeless planet or asteroid, or it might be drifting helplessly in space.Crisis Tests to bring critical systems back online can be attempted on matches of the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the following locations:

* **Power Core:** Repair the engine core.  
* **EVA:** Reroute power via external junction boxes.  
* **Engineering:** Jump-start the engines and fly away.

**Disaster:**Power spikes cause explosions, oxygen runs out, doors close suddenly crushing those in their way, etc.  
**Dimensional Rift**Whether from an experiment gone wrong, a twist of space-time, or an act of sabotage, the starship has been sucked into an alternate dimension, and reality has begun to break down.Crisis Tests to try and stabilize the ship can be attempted on matches of the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the following locations:

* **Science Lab:** Run experiments to figure out what has happened.  
* **Central Server:** Calculate your entry point for a return trip home.  
* **Power Core:** Overload the power core to punch a hole to home dimension.

**Disaster:**Reality twists causing the crew members to mutate, phase shift, or warp locations, sometimes into a solid wall.  
**Invasion**An external force has breeched the hangar bays, is boarding the ship, and has killed or captured most of the crew.Crisis Tests to engage the shipboard defenses against the invaders can be attempted on matches of the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the following locations:

* **Armory:** Unlock the weapons cases and arm the remaining crew (PCs).  
* **Security:** Program automatic defenses to identify and attack the invaders.  
* **Docking Bay:** Destroy the invaders' shuttle.

**Disaster:**A squad of invaders attacks.  
**Mutiny**Pirates or disgruntled shipmates have rebelled, taken control of the ship, and cut off all channels of communication. The remaining crew must isolate and defeat the usurpers to reclaim the ship.Crisis Tests to fight back against the mutineers can be attempted on matches of the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the following locations:

* **Security:** Retake the security room and lock down the ship.  
* **Communication:** Shutdown the mutineers' comms.  
* **EVA:** Reclaim the Command Deck.

**Disaster:**An armed squad of mutineers appears and attacks, the security system mistakes the crew for enemies, or traps and explosives trigger as the crew passes by.

#### Minor Crisis

Every session of *Starship Infernum* should also have at least one Minor Crisis at the start of play. Other Minor Crises may develop during the game depending on results from the Obstacle chart.  
An initial Minor Crisis is determined by drawing a random card from the Survival Deck and consulting the list below, or by the specific scenario selected.  
**Minor Crises do not trigger Disasters.** Any Joker drawn from the Survival Deck triggers a Disaster of the Major Crisis, no matter what crisis that Joker originally came from.  
**A) Gravity Offline**The gravity stabilizers for the ship have gone offline. Unsecured people and objects are floating throughout the rooms and hallways. No weapons may be used while this Crisis is unresolved.  
This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the *Engineering* room.  
**2\) Doors Offline**Power to the doors and passageways of the ship has been knocked offline. Doors will not open automatically and will remain open behind the characters as they explore the ship.  
This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the *Security HQ* room.  
**3\) Life Support Offline**Oxygen levels in the air are critically low. Safety rooms may not be used to recover Exhausted Traits, and players are unable to run away from Adversaries until this crisis is resolved.  
This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the *Life Support* room.  
**4\) Radiation Leak**The power core has gone critical and is throwing off massive amounts of radiation. The crew has most likely been subjected to fatal levels already. The *Radiation, Illness,* and *Cotard Delusion* obstacles begin with one round of automatic Rising Tension.

Attempts to resolve the Major Crisis may not be undertaken until this crisis is completely resolved.  
This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the *Crew Quarters* rooms.  
**6\) Starship Battle**The ship is being attacked by another ship. All *Hull Breach, External Impact,* and *Explosion* obstacles begin with one round of automatic Rising Tension.  
This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the *Weapons/Shield Control* room.  
**7\) Rogue AI**The ship's artificial intelligence has gone haywire and is now attacking the crew.  
All *Security Malfunction, Toxic Gas,* and *Electricity* obstacles begin with one round of automatic Rising Tension.  
This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the *Central Server* room.  
**8\) Experiment Gone Wrong**An experiment (or cargo) aboard the ship has gone horribly wrong. This may release an alien monster or a mob of clones, warp the ship into an alternate dimension, or turn most of the crew insane. All Adversaries are automatically one level higher.  
This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the *Science Lab*.  
**9\) Communications Down**Communications throughout the ship have been disrupted, thus preventing players in EVA suits or in separate rooms from talking to each other and coordinating their efforts.  
This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the *Communication* room.  
**10\) Engines Out**The engines on the ship have run out of fuel or broken down. The ship is dead in space and cannot move out of harm's way.  
All *Dimensional Rift, External Impact* and *Claustrophobia* obstacles begin with one round of automatic Rising Tension.  
This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the *Engineering* room.  
**J) Massive Hull Damage**A large section of the starship, comprising multiple rooms, has been completely obliterated.

This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or by performing an **EVA** to access the missing rooms.  
**Q) Dignitary Onboard**There is an important civilian passenger somewhere on your ship.  
Crew members are unable to use ranged weapons or explosives for fear of accidentally injuring the missing person. Once this crisis has been resolved and the passenger has been secured the restriction is lifted.  
This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the *Lounge* room.  
**K) Lost**The ship has been thrown off course and no one is certain where you ended up, or if you will ever be able to find your way home again. All personal Survival Tests begin with one round of automatic Rising Tension.  
This crisis may be resolved via matches on the Room & Obstacle Deck or in the *Navigation* room.

#### Encountering Adversaries

Adversaries are mobile, sentient (or semi-sentient) obstacles that the characters encounter around the ship. They should be different from game to game and relevant to the scenario and crises at hand.  
Adversaries might take the form of alien invaders, mutinous passengers, other-dimensional beasts, killer robots, or something else entirely. These are the only "thinking" obstacles in the game, and thus they are fully capable of reacting to the actions of the players, chasing them as they flee, waiting in ambush, or finding new paths through the ship to cut them off or disrupt their plans.  
Adversaries may appear as lone individuals or as a group, but all Adversaries function by the same general rules. For simplicity's sake, and to allow the Dealer to populate scenarios with enemies appropriate to the story, the Adversary column (Hearts) of the Obstacle Chart simply lists the power level of the Adversary encountered.

* **Level 1 Adversary**  
* one point of health  
* one Survival Test to defeat  
* **Level 2 Adversary**  
* two points of health  
* health may be split two ways  
* two Survival Tests to defeat  
* **Level 3 Adversary**  
* three points of health  
* health may be split three ways  
* three Survival Tests to defeat  
* may also possess an ability

The descriptions and potential abilities of various Adversaries are listed below.  
**Alter-Dimensional Entities**Adversaries from other dimensions can take many different forms; from demonic nightmares, to hyper-advanced humans, or even goatee-wearing dopplegangers of the PCs themselves.  
Accidentally crossing over to a dimension populated by these horrors, or having them slip into the "regular" world, could be a side effect of wormhole technology or strange scientific experiments.  
They might also appear due to ancient portals, intentional exploration or invasion, or even demonic rituals.  
Alter-dimensional beings might be trapped on this plane with the PCs or might flicker back and forth between the worlds. They might be able to phase out of harm's way, but they also risk becoming embedded in the bulkheads of the ship.  
Using Adversaries from other dimensions is a great way to ramp up the chthonic or demonic horror of a session, or twist the game into more of a scary psychological thriller.  
**Level 3 Ability:**The Dealer may redraw the last card dealt to them in tests against this Adversary, once per test.  
**Assimilators**Assimilators are lifeforms that will stop at nothing to achieve their ultimate goal of making everyone else just like them.  
Often these are cybernetic or even completely robotic beings, but they could also be members of a brainwashing cult or even proponents of uploading a digital consciousness to the data cloud and abandoning the physical body.  
Assimilators eliminate individuality and violently reject the notion of diversity. To them, similarity equals equality.  
**Level 3 Ability:**After the initial cards of a hand are dealt, swap the last card dealt to the Dealer with the last card dealt to the player in the Survival Test. Do this for every hand of the test.  
**Bio-Drinkers**These are opportunistic parasites that gain their sustenance by feeding on other living beings.  
Vampires are a classic example of a bio-drinker, but this category also includes aliens that drain spinal fluids or demons that feast on negative emotions and nightmares. Bio-Drinkers flip around the apex predator standing of mankind and turn the hunter into vulnerable prey.  
**Level 3 Ability:**Ties against this Adversary are considered failures instead of Pushes, and thus trigger a new round with Rising Tension. Using a Security Aptitude versus this enemy treats ties as a normal Push, and triggers a redraw without Rising Tension.  
**Cuddly Breeders**Balls of fluff that rapidly reproduce, cute looking teddy bears with sharp teeth, or little blobs of fat with smiling faces; whatever form these Adversaries take they always seem innocent and deceptively harmless.  
The true danger of these aliens is that they multiply at an alarming rate. Occasionally though, the cute exterior does in fact hide a deadly, swarming clan of enemies that will devour everything on the ship once their critical population level has been reached.  
**Level 3 Ability:**At the end of a full round (after all players in the room get to act once), if this enemy has not been defeated, it will spread to an adjacent room as a new Level 1 Adversary.  
**Drones and Robots**Drones and robots run the full spectrum of capabilities, often designed for a particular function and built to do that one job very well. However, non-living doesn't automatically mean non-thinking.  
These Adversaries can appear as self-willed machines, unreasonable murder-bots, or even former helper droids gone haywire.  
Drones are smaller robots, usually capable of flight, and are often equipped with an array of sensors and weaponry. They are rarely capable of acting beyond their strictly programed parameters and are often remote controlled in real time by a central operator.  
Robots tend to be larger, bulkier, and limited in their movement. They are less likely to have an array of abilities at their disposal and are rarely remote controlled. Instead, robots follow their coded programing and need occasional maintenance or upgrades. Robots are more likely to gain sentience or run afoul of coding quirks due to their relative independence from a human controller.  
**Level 3 Ability:**None  
**Energy Forms**These Adversaries are sentient masses of elemental energy. They generally appear as floating balls of fire, electricity, plasma, or light; but some take on a more humanoid shape, either fully engulfed with the energy or somehow keeping it constrained within a mobile shell.  
Energy forms can wreak havoc on the ship's electrical systems and are usually immune to conventional weaponry. Creative thinking will be required to completely eliminate such a foe.  
**Level 3 Ability:**If this enemy is injured in a round, but not fully eliminated, it will split into three Level 1 Adversaries that then flee in different directions.  
**Ghouls and Zombies**These Adversaries are mindless assailants that seek to devour the living or the recently deceased. Traditionally an undead creature, in a sci-fi setting these Adversaries may not actually be animated corpses at all, though most exist in a mockery of sentient life.  
Zombies and ghouls could appear as human corpses animated by a parasite or nanobots, living entities commandeered by a deadly virus, or mutated freaks driven mad by pain and suffering.  
In most occurrences, they are incredibly strong, slow moving, lack critical problem solving skills, and have a limited awareness of their surroundings.  
It has repeatedly been proven that destroying the brain of ghouls and zombies is the only way to stop them from rising again and continuing the attack.  
**Level 3 Ability:**None  
**Giant Insects**While terrestrial insects are often the subject of scientific research aboard a space faring vessel, giant insects are the stuff of nightmares.  
Giant Insects might be a naturally occurring foe, the byproduct of interstellar radiation, an escaped government project, or the result of an experiment gone wrong. Whatever their source though, they are rarely a one and done sort of threat.  
With the exception of spiders, usually when one giant insect is found there will soon be another.  
As far as tactics are concerned, insects generally have two driving needs: eat and then find a place to lay their eggs. Human bodies serve as a valid solution to either desire.  
**Level 3 Ability:**This Adversary causes two traits of damage on a bust instead of just one.  
**Greyskins**These creatures often have grey skin, ovular heads, enormous black eyes, and long spindly appendages.  
They are generally thought to be smarter and more technologically advanced than human beings and are known to abduct and perform experiments on those they view as lesser species.  
Contrarily, greyskins could be the source of first contact for humanity and may exist in the setting as mentors and crewmates, as well as potential Adversaries.  
**Level 3 Ability:**Deal two cards to the Dealer as their face-up card. The Dealer chooses one to keep and discards the other.  
**Hive Mind**This is a group of individuals that acts in coordination with a central mind, which uses each entity as an appendage of its will.  
The Hive Mind might appear as a collection of nanobots swarming over the ship, a brainwashed cult of cyborgs, or a gathering of rats that pools their mental resources.  
Hive Minds tend to look out for their own survival first and are generally not obsessed with adding new members. Instead they act as agents of rampant, hungry destruction.  
**Level 3 Ability:**When encountered at Level 3, also spawn Level 1 versions of this Adversary in all adjacent rooms. These enemies will serve as the Obstacles in unexplored rooms and add to explored ones.  
The Dealer should still draw two cards for each new room, in case of matches, but can ignore one of the cards.  
**Pirates and Scavengers**Not every crew to sail the stars is honorable or law-abiding. Pirates and Scavengers are those that will take advantage of a weakened vessel and seek to capitalize on its misfortune. Some might be aiming to kidnap the crew and pressgang them into service or sell them into slavery, but usually they would rather eliminate any witnesses to their plunder.  
These Adversaries are often equipped with a mismatched assortment of stolen tech and are usually not as skilled or regimented as career officers. This seeming disadvantage can also work to their benefit as pirates and scavengers often have less to lose and don't tend to worry about causing collateral damage.  
**Level 3 Ability:**None  
**Predatory Horror**These are the big, scary, alien monsters that stalk the shadows and pick off the crew one by one. Usually they are highly intelligent predators, capable of advanced tactics, and dissimilar to any life form encountered on Earth.  
However, if the adventure is meant to have a campy, classic TV feel to it, altering a terrestrial predator to fit the space theme is perfectly acceptable.  
For maximum threat and tension, these should usually be reserved as the basis for a Major Crisis or only as a Level 3 Adversary.  
**Level 3 Ability:**No Aptitudes may be used in tests against this Adversary.  
**Rogue Crewmates**Not everyone in the crew always has the ship's best interest in mind. There may be enterprising officers who seek to rise in rank by eliminating the competition amidst the chaos of a crisis, squads of escaped prisoners or disgruntled mutineers, or even savy saboteurs working for a rival organization.  
The most dangerous thing about rogue crewmates is that they look like everyone else aboard the ship and are often difficult to ferret out into the open.  
**Level 3 Ability:**Defeating this Adversary counts as a dead crewmate for any future tests to use a Safety room.  
**Super Brains**Super Brains are individuals of extreme intelligence that are often limited in their physical impact. They might be the classic brain-in-a-jar alien, a dangerous cloud of pure thought, or a sentient artificial intelligence gone rogue.  
Super Brains might be masters of psychic manipulation, they might be armed with laser weapons, or they could even be capable of tapping directly into the ship's defense systems.  
It is perfectly acceptable for the Dealer to keep this mastermind hidden and treat other Obstacles (such as security systems firing, explosions, or psychological breakdowns) as this Adversary's assaults on the crew. But when a Heart card is drawn, it should indicate the physical presence of the Super Brain itself and give the players a chance to defeat it.  
**Level 3 Ability:**The Dealer may redraw the last card dealt to them in case of a bust. Useable once per Test.  
**Warmongers**This class of Adversary represents those aliens biologically evolved or socially suited specifically for waging war.  
They might be trained from birth in a combat-oriented society, be generally aggressive and outfitted with natural weapons and armor, or any combination of things.  
These militant survivors should be hard to reason with and are often unafraid of death, not because they're mentally inferior, but because their lifestyle and value are often at odds with scientific or exploration-focused species. They are also generally larger, stronger, and more brutal than your average human being.  
**Level 3 Ability:**This Adversary requires four successes to fully defeat instead of the usual three.

#### Other Complications

**Why Don't The PCs Know Their Own Ship?**The basic structure of the game, via the random determination of rooms and obstacles, imposes a convention that the players do not know their way around their own ship. This concept might seem odd to some groups but can be easily managed with one of the following assumptions.  
**Only Know Their Section**The starships in *Starship Infernum* are enormous sprawling vessels that can be challenging to navigate through. Crew members each hold a specific role on the ship and may not be familiar with routes through sections that do not concern them.  
Maps, signage, and directories may be few and far between, or absent entirely on a ship that houses experimental resources or is prone to being invaded.  
**Fuzzy Memories**Due to an extended period of cryostasis, dimensional shifts, toxic gasses, or a mind-numbing illness, the players simply don't remember their way around the ship.  
This should not prevent them from knowing which rooms they have already passed through or which rooms can help resolve the Major Crisis, but they simply cannot remember how to get there.  
**Not Their Ship**The crew members don't know their way around the ship, because it isn't actually their ship. They might be stowaways or escaped prisoners, their ship may have been fundamentally altered by a trip through a wormhole or dimensional portal, or they may be exploring the remains of an unknown vessel.  
In this case, it is best to disallow the Sanitation Aptitude, as that is the one that gives the players an edge in navigating their way through their own ship.  
**Pregenerated Ship**For campaign options or scenarios where the crew most likely would know their way around, the Dealer is welcome to map out the rooms of their personal ship prior to the adventure and possibly even give a copy of the map to players. Obstacles, however, should still be drawn at random when a new room is entered.

#### PVP

*Starship Infernum* is supposed to be a game where the characters work together and do not turn against each other.  
It is almost always in the best interest of everyone's fun for the Dealer to diffuse and redirect any conflicts between the players or their characters.  
That said, situations may arise where all other options have been rejected and the only answer is to have two PCs struggle directly against one another.  
When one PC wants to act directly against the interests of another and cannot conceive of an alternate course of action, this triggers a PVP Challenge involving the affected characters.  
Any player involved in a PVP Test may relent at any time, even after cards have been dealt, and thus choose to lose the contest. This may be a wise move if a bust seems imminent.

* The Dealer deals one card face down from the Room & Obstacle Deck (not the Survival Deck as PVP should never advance the Crisis Clock) to all players involved in the challenge, plus one face down to themselves.

A second face-up card is dealt to each player and lastly to the Dealer. Players may look at their own cards at any time.  
If a player is dealt a Natural 21 they must declare it immediately.

* If only one player is dealt a Natural 21, they win the PVP challenge outright and may also refresh a Trait.

As in a Group Test, the players are still trying to beat the Dealer, not each other. However, they are attempting to beat the Dealer by a larger margin than the other players.  
Starting with the player on the Dealer's left, each player must choose to hit, stand, or use Aptitudes or Traits before the test passes on to the next player, clockwise around the table.  
**Players must announce if they have busted.** If a player busts on this test, they lose the struggle and take damage as normal.  
The Dealer must hit and stay as per the normal rules for a Group Test.

* If only one player beats the Dealer, that player wins the PVP challenge.  
* If both players fail against the Dealer, a new hand is dealt with the players in Rising Tension.  
* If the Dealer busts, or if both players beat the Dealer, the player with the highest total hand is the winner. Ties are re-dealt as normal without Rising Tension.

The winner of the PVP Challenge narrates their desired outcome.  
Damage is only assigned, as always, if a player busts on their test during the PVP Challenge.  
**Note:** Dead Crewmates may act for or against the interest of the living characters at will, including during a PVP challenge.

#### Campaign Play

*Starship Infernum* can be played as a multi-session campaign game with some very simple changes and a bit more preparation on behalf of the Dealer and the players.  
Instead of a sole crisis, campaigns could focus on a crew that explores the universe in their own starship, a salvage team breaking into derelict wrecks of old ships, or even a small squad of experts who come to the rescue of ships in trouble.  
If the campaign revolves around a single ship, that ship should be planned out ahead of time and not randomly generated during play.  
In addition, random obstacles may not be needed and the Crisis Clock could be eliminated, as the action can proceed at the story's pace like in a traditional RPG. The story ends when the players have achieved the objectives they set out to achieve, not simply when they run out of time.  
Important or recurring Adversaries can be built like characters and given Traits to affect their Test totals. However, this makes those Adversaries MUCH more dangerous and powerful, so consider this option carefully before using it.  
In a Campaign game, most players expect mechanical growth for their characters. To represent this, you may want to begin the characters with three (or less) traits all at \+/-1.  
At the end of each story arc, each PC may raise a trait by a single value up to maximum of \+/-4.  
If they no longer have any traits at \+/- 1, they may choose to gain a new trait instead of increasing an existing one.  
**Option: The Academy**The characters are students at the Galactic Academy and are training for their own future missions in space.  
Crises are manufactured events and do not result in death, if the Crisis Clock runs out, though cadets could still die if they bust too many times.  
In this campaign, players are able to recover busted traits the same way as exhausted ones *(via a Natural 21, Medkits, or Safety rooms)*.  
All Adversaries should be Level 1 and EVAs do not trigger Survival Checks for every three counters.  
The focus of an Academy campaign should be more about interpersonal relationships and less about surviving to the end.  
**Option: Rescue Team Six**The characters are an elite team of rescue operatives who are called to action when a starship is in trouble and needs immediate help.  
In many cases the crew has already been evacuated and the PCs are there to save the actual ship. This also means that they can bug out before the end, if the crisis is not resolved.  
These campaign games will play the same as a one-shot session, except for the fact that the team can abort and escape via a Hangar Bay or the Escape Pods if need be.  
Dead crewmates only have an impact for one session, and will be replaced by a new operative for the next mission unless the player of a Dead PC wants to remain as a memory for future games.  
All busted Traits are healed at the end of a mission, and every character can begin with a weapon.  
**Option: Cloning**To make for a longer game, or to provide the players with a more survivable experience, the Dealer may want to enable the use of clones or replicants. This replaces the Dead Crew member rules.  
The cloning option allows players who have been eliminated to come back to life in a *Science Lab, Medbay,* or at the *Cryopods* with no memories of what has happened thus far and one less Trait available.  
The discarded Trait may be chosen by the player and should be crossed off the character sheet. This Trait is never recoverable through the use of a Medic's ability. The clone simply never possessed the Trait to begin with.  
Clones do NOT keep a card from the hand that originally killed them, but taking advantage of a Safety room as a new clone *(or in the company of a new clone)* does require a Simple Test to see if an Exhausted Trait can be recovered.  
Clones are generally unsettling, especially to those who knew the original, at least for the first session in which they appear.

### Chapter 4: Rooms & Obstacles

Each time a character exits a room into an unexplored part of the ship, the Dealer should draw two new cards from the Room & Obstacle Deck, then compare each card to the Room & Obstacle Charts on page 50 to determine what new feature unfolds 1\.  
Not every room in the game must have an obstacle, if the Dealer prefers to give the players some breathing room 1\. It is also common for more than one room and obstacle to be in play at any given time, especially if the characters have split up 1\.  
Either card drawn may be used for the room or obstacle, unless the Sanitation Aptitude is used, in which case the player with that Aptitude chooses which is which 1\.  
If the options indicated by the chart would be too silly or stretch the credibility of the setting too far, feel free to pick another option on the chart 1\. The easiest way to do this is to move one space away in any direction 1\.  
Remember that if the values or suits of the cards drawn from the Room & Obstacle Deck match, the players will have a chance to resolve part of a crisis via a Crisis Test, once any other obstacles in that room have been dealt with 1\. Even the most inconspicuous rooms might have a computer terminal that can be hacked to allow access to a larger system 1\.  
It is possible for a starship to have more than one of the same kind of room, or for rooms to loop back to previous ones in strange ways 1\. Redundant systems may be an intentional fail safe aspect of the design, or there might need to be multiple rooms with similar functions depending on the size of the ship and the assigned crew 1\.  
If the Room & Obstacle Deck runs out of cards, simply reshuffle it and keep drawing as normal 1\.

#### Doors

Rooms on a starship are rarely connected via open entryways 1\. For the most part, doors on the ship will open automatically when a character tries to step through them and then close again behind them 1\. Some crises or scenarios remove this option, in which case doors then remain open for the rest of the game 1\.  
It is up to the Dealer to determine how many exits there are from any particular room on the ship and what form they take 2\.  
Some Rooms may have no new exits, such as storage rooms or trash compactors 2\. Dead ends such as these force the characters to backtrack through the ship (and possibly face Persistent Obstacles again) until they find a path with an unexplored option 2\.  
On a starship, travel directions are not constrained to a horizontal plane 2\. Hatches, ladders, crawlspaces, and lifts are easy ways to expand upwards or downwards through the ship 2\.

#### Making a Map

The Dealer should make a simple map as the game goes on, so that they can recall the order of the Rooms and keep track of where the Persistent Obstacles or pursuing Adversaries are 2\. One way to do this is by noting the first few letters of the room name and drawing arrows leading out from it 2\.  
As doors are opened and new cards are drawn add them to the map 2\. Ladders and lifts can be represented by a dotted line going to a new floor or an arrow leading to a new area of the page 2\.  
**Option: Nightmare Ship**  
Instead of making a map, the only rooms that are fixed in space are the ones with characters currently in them 2\.  
Each time the characters pass through a doorway, they open up an entirely new room, regardless of what was there before 2\.  
If playing with this option, it is usually more fun for the Dealer to keep the layout a secret until someone backtracks through an already opened doorway and realizes that the world is not aligned to their expectations of reality 2\.  
This option is especially suitable for games which feature supernatural horror or dimensional rifts 2\.

#### Obstacles

Obstacles are broken down into four categories based on the card suits 2\.  
**♠ Persistent (Spades)**These obstacles cannot be defeated normally, but they can be avoided or escaped 2\. Everyone in the room faces the obstacle as a group every time they enter the room again 2\. Some of these Obstacles may spread to other Rooms, if the group does not find a way to contain them 2\.  
**♥ Adversary (Hearts)**These obstacles represent sentient (or semi-sentient) beings that will attack one target at a time or will chase the PCs through the ship until the threat has been neutralized 3.The Level of the Adversary determines how many successes must be achieved on Survival Tests to completely neutralize it 3\. The full rules and options regarding Adversaries can be found on page 35 3\.  
**♦ Personal (Diamonds)**These obstacles represent psychological strain or isolated issues that assault only one of the PCs entering the room 3.If there is more than one possible target, the Dealer should deal one card to each player from the Room & Obstacle Deck 3.The player with the lowest value card (Ace is high) will then face the Survival Test 3\. If there is a tie, the players may choose who faces the test 3.Also note that many of these obstacles also have special rules that should be consulted, as they might not resolve Survival Tests by the standard rules or may have additional effects that trigger on a bust or failure 3\.  
**♣ Group (Clubs)**These obstacles can be defeated, but everyone who enters the room encounters the obstacle at the same time and must participate in a Group Test against it 3\. These are usually a one-time occurance and do not linger once they have resolved 3\.  
\*\*\* Special Effects\*\*Several rooms and obstacles trigger additional effects or have special rules 3\. These are noted on the charts and explained further under their detailed listings 3\.  
**\# Medkit**The room contains a medkit or the supplies to create one 3\.  
**† Weapons**The room contains weapons 3\. There may be enough for everyone, or their may only be a single weapon available 3\.  
**☣ Explosives**The room has the materials for creating explosives, assuming the player can succeed on a Survival Test 3\. Explosives can be used immediately in these rooms to defeat an Adversary 3\.  
**⇒ EVA**These rooms have direct access to the outside of the ship and may be used to initiate an EVA 3\.

#### Alphabetical Listing of Rooms & Obstacles

**Acid** (2♠, group, persistent, special)"As you enter this room, a sharp stinging smell burns at the lining of your nostrils 6\. Looking around, you realize that the floor is covered in pools of bubbling green acid and those pools are growing larger by the second 6\. There are patches of clear ground though; isolated sections where the acid has not yet reached 6\. If you are very lucky, and pick the exact right spots to place your steps, you might just be able to cross the room unscathed." 6  
Acid has leaked out across this room, possibly as the byproduct of an alien horror or the result of a ruptured chemical container 6\. The characters may choose not to enter this room at all and instead turn back to find another route 6\. If they choose to cross the room though, they must succeed on a Survival Test 6\.

* If the players succeed on the test, they manage to navigate the room safely 6\. This room remains hazardous should they return 6\.  
* If a player busts on the test, they slip and fall into the puddles 6\. They lose a Trait as damage as the acid either eats away at their foot or splashes up and burns them elsewhere on their body 6\. They are still able to cross the room, they just don't do it safely 6\.

Special: If a PC is wearing a spacesuit, they may choose to automatically bypass this obstacle one time, but doing so destroys the spacesuit, and it must be abandoned 6\.  
**Administration / Office** (9♣)Not every room on a massive starship can be laser beams and holodecks 6\. Sadly there has to be a place for administrative work to happen as well 6\. This is one such room 6\. It could be a private office for the Captain or First Mate, or it could be a base for Human Resources or a Diplomatic Corps 6\. Regardless, it is a necessary but often unexciting room 6\.  
The office features at least one workstation, though possibly more with a computer interface and some sort of screen 6\. Standing desks might make more sense on most spacecraft, but desks with chairs are quite common as well 6, 7\. Official looking papers secured to clipboards might be present, or files might be stored on personal tablets instead 7\. There is most likely a clock on the wall, a potted plant to breathe some life into the room, and some generic inspirational art 7\. Invariably, the thermostat controls are covered by a handwritten note that says, "Do Not Set Below 72\!" 7  
**Adversary** (2 to K♥)See page 35 for the full rules on Adversaries 7\. Remember that most Adversaries will chase the PCs from room to room, if the threat is not directly eliminated 7\.

* **Level 1 Adversary:** one Survival Test to defeat 7  
* **Level 2 Adversary:** two Survival Tests to defeat 7  
* **Level 3 Adversary:** three Survival Tests to defeat 7

**Airlock** (A♠, A♦, EVA)Airlocks are the primary method for moving people in and out of the starship and thus serve as the best location for beginning an EVA 7\. The airlock itself is usually a small pressurized chamber with airtight doors at either end of the room 7\. One door opens into the rest of the ship while the other door opens out into the cold vastness of outer space 7\. These doors can only be opened one at a time and only once the other door is secure 7\.  
Once a crewmember is in the chamber, the pressure will steadily be equalized to that of the destination to prevent damage to the ship or injury to the crewmate 7\. Spacesuits are usually found outside the airlock entrance and should be donned before entering the airlock itself 7\. In addition to the vents and valves used for pressurization, the airlock often contains computerized controls for each door, manual override wheels, and various attachment points for tethers or tools needed in a spacewalk 7, 8\. Otherwise, the airlock is empty 8\.  
**Alien Pods** (8♠, group, persistent)"This room is filled with glistening slime-covered pods that pulsate obscenely and guard a hidden life within them 8\. Standing a few feet tall, the pods are like a sticky flower bud, wrapped up tight but yearning to burst open when the time is right 8\. Occasionally one burps or gurgles, and a plume of gas escapes from the seam at the top 8\. If you are very careful, you might be able to weave your way through the nest without disturbing whatever horrors slumber inside." 8  
These pods are the eggs or pupae of slumbering baby aliens 8\. Whether collected and stored for a scientific experiment or deposited here by a bloated alien queen, they are universally disgusting and very dangerous 8\. Disturbing the pods will cause either a small alien to leap out from a pod and attack the crew or perhaps trigger a defense mechanism that attempts to ensnare the intruder and hold them as a snack until the babies have hatched 8\.  
The alien pods are too numerous to fully eliminate by attacking them directly, but they could be burned up with fire 8\. The use of explosives are bound to create a terrible and toxic mess, which will be just as much an impediment to crossing the room as the intact pods are 8\.

* If the players succeed on a Survival Test, they may make their way through the room without alerting any of the pods to their presence 8\.  
* If a player busts on the test, they are attacked and lose a Trait as a point of damage before they are able to escape 8\.

**Armory** (3♥, weapons)This is one of the most secure rooms on a starship, as this is where the small arms and anti-personnel weapons for the crew are kept, usually under careful lock and key 8\. At the very least this room will be monitored by cameras and often has a guard stationed to make sure nobody is taking weapons for unauthorized purposes 8\. The weapons stored here might even be secured by biometric or voice identification processes and user details are most likely supposed to be logged when a weapon is taken 8, 9\.  
For onboard defensive purposes, weaponry that does not disrupt the structural integrity of the ship will be most common 9\. These include basic handguns, laser, sonic, and melee style weapons 9\. Explosives, electromagnetic pulse weapons, and heavier ballistic rifles or machine guns may be available, if the crew is expected to exit the starship in a hostile or unknown environment 9\.  
**Weapons:** After defeating the obstacle in this room, each PC may choose to take either a ranged or melee weapon appropriate to the scenario and situation at hand 9\. Remember that each PC is limited to a single piece of carried equipment 9\.  
**Brig / Interrogation Room** (3♠)The brig is where prisoners on a ship are held 9\. This room could be represented by just a single room or it might be a series of small cells 9\. Spartan and purely functional, the brig will usually have a metal floor, blank walls, and little in the way of amenities 9\. For extra psychological effect, brigs are often intentionally uncomfortable and severe in appearance 9\.  
Most brigs have a security station, and the cell doors are typically controlled from there 9\. To open them, you will most likely need a key or a passcode 9\. Not all prison cells need to have barred doors and might be secured instead by a transparent forcefield or even a solid windowless panel 9\.  
The inside of a prisoner's cell will usually contain a small cot or platform for sleeping and some form of latrine 9\. Lighting will be minimal but harsh 9\. There is often a way to provide food to the prisoner without fully opening the door, and there may be restraints secured to the wall 9\. Weapons are usually not kept close to the brig in case of a prison break 9\.  
An interrogation room is much the same in form and function, with a few minor changes 9\. There won't be a bed, as this is not a room for housing a prisoner long-term, but usually there is a chair or two and possibly a table 9\. Such a room is often windowless, sound-proof, and has a solid door 9\. There may be cameras or a two-way mirror for others to witness and record what is happening inside the room without being seen in return by the prisoner 9\.  
**Cantina / Mess Hall** (2♠)Both a cantina and a mess hall serve as the primary dining place for the crew, though their feel and setup may be very different from each other 10\.  
A cantina is a smaller, more intimate space that often serves alcohol along with individually prepared meals 10\. The tables here usually seat two to six people, with comfortable chairs or as private booths 10\. The lighting and atmosphere is often conducive to socializing and relaxing, and sometimes the room even contains aquariums, stylish works of art, or decorative plants 10\. If it is more focused on serving beverages over food, then the cantina might also be dominated by a bar counter, accompanying shelves stocked with bottles, and the requisite stools 10\.  
A mess hall, by contrast, is a larger cafeteria-style space focused on serving a small selection of food in a fast and efficient manner 10\. Usually the space is dominated by long rows of tables with simple benches on either side 10\. The food is often obtained via a central queue and is served on reusable plastic trays 10\. There might be motivational posters on the walls and screens overhead running a loop of bland entertainment, which is interrupted at regular intervals by shipwide notices and reminders 10\. The lighting here is purely utilitarian, and the room can be quickly repurposed as a meeting space or triage, as needed 10\.

**Cargo Bay (9♥)**The Cargo Bay is a large room usually near the lowest levels of the ship, that contains goods or equipment securely stored for transport. These are not the supplies that a crew might need on their voyage, but are instead intended to be delivered intact to their final destination.  
Usually, this room consists of metal or plastic crates of various sizes stacked atop each other possibly with a maze of tight walkways winding between the stacks. There might also be terrestrial vehicles, pop-up instant shelters, or large pieces of equipment that are temporarily housed here as well.  
As this is a primarily utilitarian room, the walls are simple and mostly bare. There might be markings to facilitate the organization of materials and possibly safety reminders or instructions, but little thought is given to aesthetics or comfort.  
Overhead lighting is harsh and bright, and the floor is scuffed from wear and tear. There may also be ladders, tarps, nets, tie-downs, fire extinguishers, or mechanical lifting equipment found here.  
The Cargo Bay usually has a large set of sliding or rolling doors for the loading and unloading of goods. These doors are only intended to be opened when the ship has landed or is docked, and the room itself cannot be safely managed like an airlock.  
There are emergency overrides to be able to open the doors while the ship is in flight, but this will result in a rapid decompression and is only used to jettison the contents of the bay out into space.  
**Central Server (Q♥)**While most rooms on a starship will have some sort of computer interface, the central server contains the real processing power of the ship. Whether as rows of racked computers connected by a tangle of wires, all spinning and churning and dotted with blinking lights, or as a single, silent monolithic processor, this room is always thinking; so long as the power remains on.  
As important as this room is to every other system on a starship, it is usually also one of the most secure. There is rarely more than one door leading in and out of the room, though this door might take the form of an airlock-style passage or security checkpoint. The server room also needs to be kept cool, since the machines inside can easily overheat, and must be free of dust and debris.  
There are unlikely to be any desks or workstations in the central server room, though there may be monitors to check the status of various processes.  
There might also be a large display or holographic projector equipped with microphones and speakers to facilitate a more human-like interaction with a shipboard AI.  
**Classroom / Training Room (J♣)**The purpose of this space is to teach a new skill or to brush up on old ones. While it takes many different forms, depending on the particular subject needed, the function of the room is always the same.  
A traditional classroom setup has rows of desks facing the front of the room where a podium, screen, or white board is located. Most likely each desk is equipped with their own computer interfaces and a standard chair or stool.  
There might be informational charts or posters on the wall and possibly a row of cabinets for classroom supplies.  
A technical training center is set up quite differently than a basic classroom, as the skills being taught are of a more practical and hands on variety. Here there will be working replicas of ship stations, mechanical components, and various tools.  
Training that typically involves dangerous situations, such as emergency engineering procedures or spacewalks, most likely happens in simulator rooms or holodecks. Other advanced training could take place in the actual working conditions, such as in the clinic for medical staff.  
Lastly, a shooting range or martial arts dojo should also be considered a training room, though again it differs greatly in design from the previous two.  
In these cases, the focus is on practicing personal defense skills in a controlled environment or trying out new techniques on friendly opponents.  
The shooting range should have sound dampeners, designated lanes for each trainee, and variable stationary or moving targets within each lane.  
A room for training hand to hand combat consists of a padded floor, targets for punching and kicking, practice weaponry, and possibly a wall of mirrors.  
**Claustrophobia (2♦, personal)**"No matter how big it is, the starship is still a constrained space. That's never been more clear than now, in the midst of an emergency. There is nowhere to escape to; no true freedom to be had. Even in the vastness of space, you are trapped. As this truth sounds over and over again in the depths of your brain, your breathing becomes fast and frantic. The walls begin to close in on you, sweat breaks out on your brow, and your knees begin to buckle under the mental strain of your imprisonment."  
The character is suffering from a sudden and extreme bout of claustrophobia. If they don't pull it together, they are bound to do something dangerous.

* **If the player succeeds on this Survival Test,** they manage to hold on to their sanity and push the rising panic down again.  
* **If the player busts,** they freak out and either cause a mental crack or actually do something that causes physical damage to themselves. Either way, they lose a Trait as damage.

**Command Bridge (5♠)**Arguably the coolest room in the entire starship, this is where the Captain and other officers monitor their vessel and provide direction for the crew.  
At a minimum, the bridge contains the steering and propulsion controls for the ship; screens, viewports, or other indicators of the outside environment; and a command station for the Captain. In addition, it often houses a communication array, weapons controls, and status readouts for the other critical systems of the starship.  
In ships based on traditional sailing vessels, the Captain and pilot face the front, or bow, of their ship with the rest of the officers arrayed around them. A window or large screen dominates the wall in front of them.  
In starships based on other designs, such as in a submarine, a windowless bridge might be buried deep in the ship, with the various stations arrayed in a circle around the Captain.  
In practical terms, the bridge can face any direction and its location does not need to be limited by a naval tradition. What is important is that the bridge serves as the central control point for the commanding officers of a vessel and can usually communicate with every other room on the starship.  
**Communication (2♦)**The communication room is dominated by computers dedicated to receiving external radio waves, but the room may also contain the equipment for recording audio and video as well.  
Typically this room contains several rows of workstations with monitors, scanners, and radar screens constantly analyzing and relaying new information. Most stations are equipped with headphones or a speaker array for public listening.  
A switchboard to handle onboard communications and a microphone for shipwide announcements are standard. In addition, there might be equipment for translating exotic languages or deciphering other complicated transmissions.  
If this room also serves as a media production facility, there will be additional microphones, a camera, a green screen, and possibly even a holographic projector as well as the machines needed to compile, edit, store, and transmit the recorded material as needed.  
**Contamination (8♣, group)**"There is loud pop, like a cork bursting out of a bottle, followed by a steady hiss. Strange liquid begins to spray out in an arc over your head, though it certainly isn't champagne. A pipe on the ceiling has sprung a major leak and you are directly in the path of whatever horribly nasty chemical it had been carrying."  
The inner workings of a starship contain many caustic chemicals that keep the various systems running; acids to help break down waste, hydraulic fluid in the doors, liquid hydrogen in the fuel cells, and bleach in the water treatment, just to name a few.  
Normally these chemicals are safely in their valves, pipes, and tubes but occasionally they spring a leak; which is exactly what has happened here.  
Thankfully, after this test is resolved, if there is power to the ship the leak will automatically be rerouted.  
If there isn't power, the pressure will fade away after a minute or two.

* **If the players succeed on the Survival Test,** they manage to jump out of the way or shield themselves from the spray.  
* **If a player busts on the test,** their character gets a facefull of chemicals, causing serious burns or even temporary blindness and they lose a Trait as damage.

**Note:** Players wearing a spacesuit are immune to the Contamination obstacle.  
**Cotard Delusion (8♦, personal, special)**"It suddenly hits you that all of this is a lie. What is it that makes you so special? What is it that makes you able to survive when others have died? The answer hammers at the back of your brain. A single word, over and over again. 'Nothing\!' You are nothing\! You are an insignificant speck. You didn't survive... You are already dead. And this? This is just what comes after."  
**Special:** This obstacle is a single-hand Survival Test with no Rising Tension.

* **If the player succeeds on the test,** they push the dark thoughts away and do not succumb to the delusion.  
* **If the player fails the test,** they will believe themselves to be actually deceased. This belief is so strong that they will assume the next obstacle they face cannot possibly hurt them. They will automatically begin their next Survival Test with two rounds of Rising Tension.  
* **If a player busts on this Survival Test,** they freak out and cause an injury to themselves in order to determine if they are actually alive or dead.

**Crewmate (A♣, group, special)Special:** The players suddenly discover a fellow crewmate in trouble. This is not another PC, but is a random non-PC suitable to the scenario.  
Draw a second obstacle card from the Room & Obstacle Deck to determine what kind of trouble the non-PC is facing.

* **If the players succeed on this test,** they have helped save the crewmate and escaped from or defeated the second obstacle.  
* **If the players fail on this test,** they do not trigger Rising Tension. However, the crewmate is killed and the PC(s) must now test against the second obstacle by themselves.  
* **If a players busts on this test,** the crewmate is killed and the PC is injured by the second obstacle, thus losing a Trait as damage.

**Note:** The players may choose to ignore the crewmate in need, and thus the extra obstacle, by leaving the room the way they entered. If they do this, the crewmate will be killed and the dead non-PC will count as a deceased PC crewmate for the purpose of resting at a Safety room.  
**Crew Quarters (7♠♦)**The details of individual crew quarters differ from ship to ship, but the function of this room remains the same no matter the mission; to provide a place for the crew to sleep.  
At its most basic, the crew quarters is a communal space that consists or cots or bunk beds set into the walls or otherwise arranged in a regimented manner. Individual beds might even be shared on a shift by shift rotation. There is no expectation of privacy in this type of quarters, though a secure locker or crate is often provided for personal belongings.  
At the other end of the spectrum is a private room that houses just one or two crewmates or serves as a family dwelling.  
These spaces might still be cramped and spartan, containing only the necessities, or they may feel more like a hotel room or apartment with some attention paid to comfort and personalization. These types of quarters often have attached bathroom facilities, adjustable lighting, built-in personal storage, and a small workstation. They are also usually decorated to suit the occupants with letters from home or favorite pieces of art.  
**Cryopods (10♣)**The dominant feature of this room is the coffin-like pods used to keep their occupants in a stable state of suspended animation. These are most often used for extremely long voyages where the speed and tedium of travel would negatively impact the human body.  
While this room is noted as Cryopods, this does not mean that the bodies are necessarily frozen. This is simply the term that has gained the most prominent recognition despite the actual process used for preservation.  
The pods themselves are usually placed one by one along the walls leaving the central space in the room empty. There is usually a tangle of tubes and wires running from each pod to other equipment and at least one computer used to monitor and control the conditions inside the pods.  
The pods tend to be arranged in a vertical orientation with the occupants standing, as the idea of being laid down in a container is too reminiscent of death.  
The pods might be full of womb-like fluid, which needs to drain away as the occupant is awakened, or they might involve slowly lowering the temperature of the body and replacing the internal fluids so that actual freezing does not occur. In this case, the pod would be empty of suspension material, but the body may have a crystalline look and be covered in frost.  
Conversely, there might not be any pods at all. Instead the transported persons could be suspended in solid matter, or even stored as nothing more than raw data or pure energy. No matter the process, there tends to be a period of disorientation and weakness once a person is revived.

This room is filled with dangerous debris. Whether floating weightless through the air or covering the ground, it makes crossing the room a hazardous experience.  
The debris could be represented by jagged crystals of ice, broken shards of glass, twisted ruins of metal, the shrapnel of a recent explosion, or even small explosives themselves that activate when touched or jostled.

* **If the players succeed on their Survival Tests,** they manage to traverse the room without issue.  
* **If a player busts on their test,** they have set off a mine or stumbled into something sharp and thus lose a Trait as damage.

* **If the players succeed on this Survival Test,** they manage to navigate the room without bumping into anything that could cause them injury.  
* **If a player busts,** they do exactly the opposite and lose a Trait of damage. Perhaps they cut themselves on something sharp, or they manage to trip over an errant cargo crate. Or maybe they knock something dangerous off the counter and cause an explosion.

After this test is resolved, if there is power to the ship, the lights can be turned back on. If there is currently no power, the players will have to come up with another way to provide illumination, such as the small flashlights built into most spacesuits.  
**Dimensional Rift (10♠, group, persistent)**"A jagged tear runs across the floor and up the wall of this room. Not a rip in the metal of the ship, but a hole in the very fabric of reality. Somehow, be it through a natural weak spot in the universe or a scientific experiment gone wrong, the barrier between dimensions has been torn asunder, and evil things are leaking through.  
While there might be other dimensions that are harmless or even pleasant, this rift doesn't lead to one of those places. Instead it leads to a nightmare world, a place of either shadow, or a hell.  
There is no easy way to seal this rift, though it may be isolated from causing further damage by the use of field dampeners or other fancy science tech.  
**Note:** If this obstacle does not make sense given the setting or tone of the current scenario, feel free to treat it as a Hull Breach instead.

* **If the players succeed on this Survival Test,** they manage to break free of the rift without ill effects.  
* **If a player busts on the test,** their body is wracked by otherworldly forces, and they lose a Trait as damage.

**Double (A♠, special)Special:** This room is extra dangerous. Draw two more cards from the Room & Obstacle Deck and generate obstacles from each.  
Players must make separate tests against each obstacle in this room.

**Electric Discharge (7♣, group)**"This room is alive with the buzz of electricity. Like a thousand angry bees, power surges and sparks from panel to panel along the walls and floor, but the rubber in your boots should keep you safe while you make your way across. The problem seems to originate from a broken computer terminal on the opposite side of the room. As you make your way towards it, a tingling sensation suddenly runs up your leg and warns you all too late that the trust in your shoes was sorely misplaced."  
Electrical current is running through almost everything on the starship, but it is usually contained and well insulated.  
In this case something electrical has become broken, ungrounded, or is overloaded with power and throwing off sparks. The voltage is high enough to send a person flying backwards across the room, and the current is strong and steady enough to kill them.

* **If the players succeed on their Survival Tests,** they are able to avoid getting zapped and possibly even manage to shut down the threat.  
* **If a player busts on the test,** they are instead shocked with a tremendous amount of electricity and lose a Trait as damage. If they are carrying tools or weapons with electrical components, then these are shorted out and ruined as well.

**Engineering (8♠)**Engineering is the mechanical heart of the starship, as this is the room which ensures the vessel is capable of controlled movement through space. While it may not contain the actual engines and even more rarely contains the fuel for those engines, this room does house the equipment, connections, and controls that make it all work.  
In most vessels, this room is a maze of large machines, valves, and conduits all accessed by various hatches, ladders, and catwalks.  
While there may be a central computer used to monitor the overall condition of the engines and quickly respond to changes, most components of the complex system also have their own gauges and localized controls.  
Unless the ship is brand new, there are most likely many work arounds, quick fixes, and creative solutions that only the engineering team would fully be able to navigate.  
There are also ways to close off or isolate damaged subsystems and in many cases multiple redundancies of crucial equipment.  
Tools are supposed to be located in a specific central storage place, but are usually scattered around within easy reach of where they are most often needed.  
**Escape Pods (8♣, medkit)**Escape Pods are the small shuttles attached to the outside of the ship for use in an emergency evacuation. They are usually cramped and efficient, with every inch of space intended to be occupied by tools, supplies, or a passenger.  
These pods are stocked with a temporary shelter, a few days worth of food and drinkable water, an emergency medical kit, flotation devices, basic survival tools, and a radio beacon. Escape pods rarely contain extra EVA suits, but these may be located in the main ship just outside.  
While this room may indicate a bay with multiple escape pods, there is only ever a single point of entry to the pods themselves and their doors must be sealed before the craft can separate from the mothership.  
Launching a pod is often initiated by an explosive charge which propels the pod away in a set trajectory. Some escape pods may have basic propulsion and directional controls, but most will simply float in space or crash land on a nearby planet, moon, or asteroid and await rescue.  
**Medkit:** After the obstacle inside or blocking the escape pods is resolved, one single-use medkit may be retrieved from inside the pod.  
**Exhaustion (9♦, personal, special)**"It feels like you've been going full tilt for hours, and your body is starting to ache from the strain. Despite the high stakes of survival, you simply can't maintain this adrenaline rush forever. You need to stop and take a break, or at least slow down for a few minutes and catch your breath. It won't do you any good to survive the current disaster only to drop dead from exhaustion after."  
The targeted character is worn out and ready to drop. Push on as they will, eventually the stress is going to take a toll on them.  
**Special:** This obstacle is a single-hand Survival Test with no Rising Tension.

* **If the player succeeds on the test,** they manage to catch their breath for a moment and may carry on without penalty. This is not the same as a Safety, however, and they do not get to recover an Exhausted Trait.  
* **If the player fails the Survival Test,** they are exhausted and will be unable to use any Traits or special abilities on the next test they face.  
* **If the player busts on the test,** they are so exhausted that they drop to the floor, panting for breath as every muscle in their body screams in pain, and they lose a Trait as damage.

**Explosion (6♣, group)**"Ka-booom\! A deafening sound and the accompanying shockwave of force ripples through the ship. Bright white light, searing fire, and jagged bits of shrapnel fly outward from the blast. You don't time to think. You don't have time to scream. If you are lucky, you might have just enough time to hit the deck."  
There are lots of reasons things might explode on a starship. Volatile chemicals mix together by accident, an errant spark ignites the oxygen rich atmosphere, missiles or laser cannons slam the ship, or a bomb is planted by an intentional saboteur. Whatever the reason the characters are unfortunately caught in the blast.

* **If the players succeed on this Survival Test,** they manage to take cover and avoid the worst of the blast.  
* **If a player busts on this test,** they are hit by the explosion, or shell-shocked by the blast, and must lose a Trait as damage.

**External Impact (3♣, group)**"There is a sickening, stomach flipping crunch as something big and heavy strikes the outer hull of the ship. You are knocked off your feet as the metal overhead crumbles and the entire room quakes with the force of the impact. Arms flailing, you reach out to grab hold of anything that might slow your fall."  
Something has struck the exterior of the ship and caused significant damage to the hull. This might be a large asteroid, a flying piece of space junk, or even a blast from an enemy vessel.  
This alone is bad enough, but to make matters worse, the point of impact is the room currently occupied by the characters. While they are certain to fall and possibly injure themselves, there is also a risk of being crushed by the impact.

* **If the players succeed on their Survival Test,** they manage to keep their footing, stop their fall, or dive out of the way without injury.  
* **If a player busts on their test,** they injure themselves by falling and lose a Trait as damage.

**Note:** Characters currently outside the ship on an EVA must also participate in the group test when this obstacle is drawn for any inside room. This test does NOT replace the regular EVA Survival Tests.  
**Extreme Pressure (6♠, group, persistent)**"There is no obvious reason, at first, for why your head starts to pound and your vision goes blurry. Then your face goes numb and your joints begin to pop, as objects around the room burst open or shatter. You realize that for some unknown reason this room is experiencing a significant rise in atmospheric pressure, and if you don't get out of here fast you too are going to implode."  
The pressure in this room keeps rising and there isn't really any way to stop it. This effect is the reverse of a vacuum, causing things inside the room to be crushed if they cannot withstand the forces being exerted.  
The players can attempt to resist the pressure long enough to repair a system in this room, but they will need to test again for every round they remain in these conditions.  
This is an isolated effect and will not spread to new locations.

* **If the players succeed on their Survival Tests,** they have managed to escape the room in time or are pushing their way past the effects and may linger for a round.  
* **If a player busts on this test,** the pressure has affected them enough that they begin vomiting and lose a Trait as damage.

**Extreme Temperatures (9♠, group, persistent)**"As you open the door to this room, you are struck by a wave of sweltering heat. You gasp for breath as beads of sweat break out across your body. The room isn't on fire, at least not yet, but you can see things in the room melting through the shimmering haze. If you linger too long, you will most likely start melting as well."  
It doesn't have to be heat in this room, as extreme cold is just as likely and just as dangerous, but whether hot or cold the temperatures are beyond what the human body can handle.  
There is no quick and easy solution for this threat, though creative thinking should be rewarded if the players come up with a way to neutralize it.  
Instead, the best thing the players can hope for is to cross the room without succumbing. If they choose to linger, the players must make a Survival Test each round to remain without taking damage.

* **If the players succeed on their Survival Tests,** they manage to cross this room without serious repercussions.  
* **If a player busts on this test,** they lose a Trait as damage. This might be psychosomatic with real wounds appearing on the character's body because their mind insists that they were injured, or it might be the result of a character ignoring hazards in the actual environment by focusing on the illusory one.

**Garden / Hydro (Q♣)**This room may serve as a place to relax as well as a production facility, as it could be arranged to simulate a park or other natural space reminiscent of Earthly gardens.  
By contrast, a hydroponic farm can make more use of the space by allowing rows of plants to grow in vertically stacked containers and up and down the walls.  
In either case, plants are most likely harvested by robots, though humans may choose to assist with the gardening as a leisure pastime.  
**Gym (5♣)**The primary function of this room is to provide exercise and recreation for the crew aboard the ship. This room can appear in varied forms and configurations depending on the specifics of the ship and the needs of the crew.  
In its most basic form, the gym serves as a workout room with treadmills, stationary bikes, rowing machines, and weight training equipment. There might be music playing or entertainment provided via screens on the walls or built into the machines themselves. This option makes the most efficient use of a limited space.  
For more specialized rooms, the gym could take the form of a bowling alley, swimming pool, or boxing ring. Handball, tennis courts, or basketball courts are also possible, as is a rock climbing wall or any combination of these things.  
**Hallucination (3♦, personal, special)**"With a startled gasp, you jump back and stare at the empty space before you. There stands an old friend from a long time ago. They shouldn't be here\! It isn't right. It isn't safe. As you open your mouth to demand an explanation, your friend pulls a gun from inside their jacket, aims it at you, and pulls the trigger."  
The hallucination is a mental illusion of another dangerous obstacle, however no new card needs to be drawn to determine the specifics of it. Whatever the character imagines they are seeing, even though nobody else in the room will see it as well, is the obstacle at hand.

* **If a character succeeds on this Survival Test,** they realize the falsehood of the illusion and force it from their mind.  
* **If a character busts,** they lose a Trait as damage. This might be psychosomatic with real wounds appearing on the character's body because their mind insists that they were injured, or it might be the result of a character ignoring hazards in the actual environment by focusing on the illusory one.

**Special:** Due to the personal nature of a Hallucination, splits are not allowed on this obstacle.  
**Hallway (4♠♥♦♣)**Hallways are the passages that connect rooms of the ship together and provide the means for efficiently moving crew and equipment to various locations. Most hallways on large vessels are big enough for several people to walk side by side, and for traffic to flow in both directions.  
On military ships however, the corridors may be cramped and difficult to traverse in an effort to preserve the space for other essential components and to prevent enemies from freely moving about if the ship is boarded.  
**Hangar Bay (8♥, EVA)**The Hangar Bay is a place for smaller ships and shuttlecraft to enter and exit the much larger starship. On a military vessel, this will most likely serve as the staging area for multiple small fighters and bombers; while on an exploration, rescue, or science vessel, this room might house just a single shuttle craft. Regardless of the scope and scale of a hangar bay, they should all have the same basic features.  
Every hangar bay contains a way to exit the ship but still protects the inside of the room from the ravages of space.  
This could take the form of double bay doors that slide open to release the smaller craft once the hangar has been sealed and pressurized, it might be an open hole in the ship protected by a forcefield, or it could even be launch tubes that the individual ships drop into before being shot out into space.  
Hangar bays contain emergency fire fighting equipment and tools to maintain and repair the ships, as well as ladders, helmets, and extra flight suits. There will also be painted markings on the floor used along with color coded lights and directions from a ground crew to help guide and organize multiple ships.  
On a military vessel, there is usually a defensive turret to guard the bay doors and a stockpile of ammunition for the smaller attack ships.  
**Hologram (K♣, group, special)Special:** The Hologram is an illusion of another obstacle. Instead of drawing a second obstacle card, you may use the room card already drawn to determine the illusion or you may simply choose another obstacle that makes sense in the given room.

* **If a characters succeed on this Survival Test,** they realize that the obstacle is an illusion and are able to ignore it.  
* **If a player busts on the test,** then the illusion will be shattered and subsequent players do not need to face it. The character who busted has somehow managed to injure themselves in their reaction, possibly by running into something dangerous that was concealed by the illusion, and they lose a Trait as damage.

**Hull Breach (5♠, group, persistent, EVA, special)**"The moment you open the door to this room, you realize that something is terribly wrong. You, and everything else around you, are sucked forwards and swept towards the gaping hole in the side of the ship. Beyond that hole you can see the inky black emptiness of space; a lonely grave awaiting your frozen, drifting corpse. You need to seal this room and fast, but first you have to find something stable to hold on to."  
A piece of the hull is missing, and thus this room is open to the void of outer space.  
This obstacle can already be in existance when the door to the room is opened, or something may happen once the PCs are in the room to cause the hull to be ripped away. Either way, there is no solution to patch the hole. The PCs' only option is to hang on tight and try to escape.

* **If the players succeed on their Survival Test,** they have managed to hang on and drag themselves to safety, assuming that they can also seal off the room and stop the depressurization of their ship.  
* **If a player busts on the test,** they have been hit by a flying piece of debris and lose a Trait as damage. Unless this is their last Trait, they do manage to also hang on and escape.

**Special:** This is a persistent obstacle that has the potential to affect other rooms if there is a clear and open route from them to the Hull Breach. If the room containing the Hull Breach is sealed off from the rest of the ship, it will no longer pose serious threat.  
But if the doors cannot be closed for some reason, such as the power is out or there are no doors to this room to begin with, then this obstacle must also be tested against for any occupied room in the chain and must be retested each time a character moves to an impacted room.  
Players who are in a spacesuit and pass the Survival Test for the Hull Breach before the situation is resolved may also choose to be sucked out of the ship but grab onto the hull, thus beginning an EVA on their next turn.  
**EVA:** Characters may attempt an EVA from this room, once the room has been stabilized and sealed off from the rest of the ship, by treating the adjacent room like an airlock.  
**Laundry (7♣)**At first glance this room doesn't provide much excitement, as it exists to handle the washing, drying, and repair of linens, towels, and uniforms. In fact, on many ships automated driers, futuristic fabrics, and a lack of bedsheets may eliminate the need for anything other than an occasional washing of clothes.  
However, this is also a potentially wet and hazardous location with plenty of places to hide.  
This room could be a self-serve facility, packed full of small washers that accomodate a single load at a time, or it could be an automated dry cleaner-type service where clothes are hung on a hook and then conveyed through a series of machines.  
There might be push carts for transporting dirty laundry, heated irons or steam machines, and most likely some mildly caustic cleaning chemicals.  
**Library (Q♠)**The library serves as a repository of knowledge on the ship, whether that is in physical books and maps or as intangible data files. Most libraries are well lit and kept quiet in order to facilitate easier reading and enhanced concentration. Also, there is often a trained crewmember stationed in the library to assist others in their research or to answer questions.  
If book-based, the library will have shelves for organizing the books, as well as tables and lecterns, and several computers for other media. Even if mostly digital, the library will still have comfortable chairs for reading and will most likely be fitted with sound-dampening wall panels and a soft carpet.  
Between the stacks or under the tables there may be suitable hiding places for crewmates attempting to escape pursuing Adversaries.  
**Lift (A♥♣)**While there may be the occasional ladder leading from one deck to another, stairs are almost unheard of on a starship. Instead, elevators or lifts are used to move from one level to another. Usually these are large enough to hold several crew members at once and contain an array of buttons for various destinations.  
Lifts are an excellent way to split off the map and give the ship a sense of size, as most lifts will service multiple floors and allow for extended exploration.  
Many Obstacles encountered with a lift can be held until a button inside is pressed. Once the doors are closed, the characters are trapped and completely at the mercy of whatever complication will befall them.  
It is much more dramatic to spring a trap that they cannot avoid than to give them a hint of what awaits should they choose to climb inside.  
**Life Support (2♥)**The life support functions onboard a starship are created by a series of independent systems working together to provide clean water and a comfortable, breathable atmosphere. This room houses the machines that make this possible. Sometimes the room will also contain reclamation and recycling equipment, emergency oxygen reserves, and possibly even fire suppression systems.  
While the massive tangle of pipes, vents, and equipment in this room works mostly on its own, it must be regularly maintained.  
Filters must be changed, intakes unclogged, and samples taken to ensure the lack of contamination. As this is one of the most critical rooms on a ship, it is also a perfect target for sabotage.  
**Lockers / Bathroom (J♦)**Locker rooms and bathrooms are another of those dull but necessary suites of rooms that can be put to creative and dramatic use.  
They might be communal affairs, like in a military barracks or an athletic club, or they might be facilities that provide a good bit of privacy. Either way, sightlines are often severely limited and these rooms offer many places to hide or to be ambushed.  
On a starship, where conservation of resources is a premium consideration, showers and toilets will use a minimal amount of water, if any at all.  
Other options include jets of steam, bacteria killing powders or mists, waterless gels, or even vacuums and laser arrays. Forced air dryers make more sense than towels on most starships.  
As there is something psychologically satisfying about being able to soak and submerge, there might be bathing tubs, whirlpools, and saunas available to anyone on the crew. Or these may be amenities that are only be found in the quarters those with higher status or rank.  
**Lounge (10♥)**The lounge is a room intended for entertainment and relaxation. This can be styled as a movie theatre for recorded entertainment or may contain a stage for live performances. It may also be more casually arranged for socializing or listening to music.  
The primary feature of the lounge is its array of comfortable seating. Cushioned chairs are either lined up to face the stage or screen or gathered together in clusters around small round tables.  
The room is often dimly lit from wall sconces and lamps instead of overhead fixtures, though the stage will be brightly illuminated when in use.  
Most lounges contain a bar or sideboard for finger foods and drinks. Depending on the nature of the ship, there might even be alcohol allowed in here for off duty personnel. There is often more than one exit from the lounge and plenty of shadowy places to hide.  
**Maintenance Tunnel (6♥♣, special)**Maintenance tunnels weave unseen through the ship, providing access to spaces and equipment not normally serviced by traditional corridors.  
Unlike a hallway, these tunnels are usually low ceilinged, cramped, cluttered, and rarely lit. Tubes and wires criss cross overhead, boxes and beams extend randomly into the space, and vents or trapdoors suddenly appear at irregular intervals underneath.  
Maintenance tunnels make for excellent places to hide, but due to the care needed to travel safely through them, they are not an ideal option for moving quickly or escaping pursuit.  
Spacesuits are unable to be worn in maintenance tunnels for the same reason. There is simply too much that can get in the way.  
**Special:** A maze of maintenance tunnels connect most rooms on the ship, albeit in a roundabout manner. Characters may use the tunnels to attempt to access a specific room of their choice.  
As in an EVA, the character must make a Simple Test each round of travel to progress towards their destination. Once they have succeeded on three such tests, they arrive where they wanted to go.  
However, if they fail the Simple Test, they must stop and face a new obstacle before being able to proceed. Failure does not eliminate previous successes.  
**Medbay (9♠♦, medkit)**This room can serve as a doctors office, dental clinic, operating theatre, triage, infirmary, and in dire situations, a morgue.  
Though arranged and equipped differently depending on the particular function desired, all of these various rooms are used for treating the sick and injured.  
Most medical rooms will have a tall operating or examination table or in some cases a reclining chair. They will also be well lit by bright overhead lamps that can be repositioned as needed. Many will feature a rolling stool for the attending physician, as well as carts for instruments and wheeled gurneys for transporting the seriously ill or incapacitated.  
These rooms are usually stocked with the tools and supplies needed for medical practice, including scanners, syringes, scopes, x-ray machines, splints, surgical lasers and scalpels, tweezers, anesthetics, and other drugs for emergency or prescription use.  
**Medkit:** After all obstacles are defeated in the Medbay, any character can assemble one single-use medkit from the supplies in this room.  
**Microblast (5♦, personal)**"The hairs on the back of your neck stand on end and some inner sense causes you to hesitate for just a moment. Then the world explodes, or at least a small part of it does\! A blossom of fire and noise rushes out at you and in your heightened state, it almost seems to move in slow motion. But you seem to be moving slowly too, desperately pulling backwards in a vain attempt to escape the flames."  
This obstacle represents a small explosion centered on just one character. It can be targeted normally, by dealing a card to everyone from the Room & Obstacle Deck and having it affect the person with the lowest card, or it can go off on the first person to touch something in the room.

* **If the character succeeds on the Survival Test,** they manage to pull back in time and are only startled and singed.  
* **If the character busts on the test,** they are unable to dodge out of the way and lose a Trait of damage as they are blasted with the full force of the explosion.

**Minor Crisis (K♠♦, special)**A new Minor Crisis has suddenly occurred on the starship.  
**Special:** Draw the top card from the Survival Deck and place it face up next to the existing Crisis cards. Consult the Crisis chart to see what new complication has affected the ship, but do NOT place a Joker on top of this new card. Lastly, add a token back to the Crisis Clock. The players now have one more Reel to deal with their compounding problems.  
This new crisis is not considered an obstacle, and this room now triggers no Survival Tests (though it is not converted to a Safety).

**Navigation (3♦)**While most starships can be piloted quite successfully from the bridge, this specialized room contains the maps and computers to handle more tricky calculations. Unlike navigation on land, objects in space are constantly moving, and this must be taken into account when plotting a course through the galaxy.  
Viewscreens and readouts lining the walls display the ship's current coordinates, rate of speed, and trajectory as well as similar data for other ships and asteroids nearby.  
Physical or holographic star charts are close at hand, perhaps on a central table, and several computers are constantly updating the position of obstacles in the ship's path while sending real time course corrections both to the engines and the bridge.  
There is most likely nothing in navigation to use as a reliable weapon. While there may be multiple exits from this room, there are also not any good places to hide.  
**Noise (10♣, group)**"After a few steps into this room, your presence has certainly been noticed. Alarms on the walls start to flash, and ear-splitting sirens begin to sound. This obstacle represents not just a loud, persistent noise but a sound so relentless that it can actually damage the characters' bodies or psyche.

* **If the players succeed on their Survival Tests,** they manage to escape the noise, withstand the assault, or (depending on the narration of their actions) shut it down at the source.  
* **If a player busts on the test,** they lose a Trait as damage, either by shattering their psyche, becoming nauseated by the sound, or actually injuring themselves in an attempt to escape.

**Observation Deck (K♥)**The observation deck is a large room with huge windows or view screens that allow the people onboard the ship to gaze out at the wonders of space.  
This room serves no real tactical purpose, as there is very little quality data not already gathered by the ship's probes and scanners that could be gleaned from simple visual observations, but it does provide a unique place to relax or contemplate weighty matters.  
Most people prefer to stroll through this space, or to simply stand in one spot for a while, though there are the occasional benches to accommodate longer stays. The room is usually dimly lit and quiet except for the everpresent hum of the engines.  
If windows span from floor to ceiling, or wrap above and below the viewers, safety railings are most likely provided as well. Not out of concern for anyone falling out of the ship, but to provide a solid sense of grounding when confronted with the overwhelming panorama.  
There is absolutely nowhere to hide in this room and nothing that could be used as a weapon. The room is quite simply just a big open space.  
**Officer Quarters (7♥)**Similar to regular crew quarters, the officer quarters are designed to provide a place to sleep and unwind from the the stress of command. They are usually nicer than a regular crew berth in terms of comfort and amenities and are most often set up for individual officers. Unless officers are married or have a family, it is unlikely that they will share their quarters with another person on the ship.  
These quarters always have a bed, perhaps larger than those for the general crew, and contain a small desk or computer workstation, wardrobe or closet, and usually a small private bathroom facility.  
As a private space, it is common to find books, pictures of family, and often a personal supply of food or alcohol. There will also be art that reflects the particular occupant's personal taste.  
There is rarely more than one entrance to the room, and if there is a pistol or other weapon here it will be hidden and secured.  
**Oxygen Critical (Q♣, group)**"At first, everything seems normal in this room. Then you start to feel a little bit dizzy. The air here is all wrong, and you are clearly about to suffocate."  
Whether from a fault in the life support system or through a quirk of the seals on this room, there simply isn't enough breathable air to go around in here.  
This is a good obstacle to hold until the PCs have started exploring the room or are attempting to fix a system, thus allowing the threat to creep up on them.  
After the initial Survival Test, clever players should be able to remedy the situation and make the room safe again.

* **If the players succeed on their Survival Test,** they are able to keep functioning long enough to escape the room or restore a breathable atmosphere.  
* **If a player busts on this test,** they have passed out and lose a Trait as damage, either through a permanent brain injury due to lack of oxygen or by falling and causing trauma to their body.

**Note:** Androids or those wearing spacesuits are immune to the effects of this obstacle.  
**Panic Attack (Q♦, personal)**"What were you even thinking? You are trapped inside a metal tube hurtling through space with nowhere to run and nowhere to hide. The chances of survival are insanely low. Wait\! What's that noise? Something is pounding\! Boom, boom, boom\! The ship is breaking apart\! You are trapped\! You are all going to die in here\!"  
Strangely enough, this room is actually safe from external threats. That doesn't stop the built up stress from finally overwhelming the targeted character though.  
The affected character starts to freak out and has to try and bring themselves back under control.

* **If the player succeeds on this Survival Test,** they do manage to hold it together and calm down.  
* **If the player busts on this test,** they freak out and lose control, perhaps having a mild heart attack or injuring themselves in the midst of their panic. They also lose a Trait as damage.

**Note:** This test cannot be spit on doubles to allow another player to intervene.  
**Paranoia (4♦, personal, special)**"The rest of the crew has been looking at you funny. Nobody has said anything, at least not directly, but you realize that they have been plotting your demise all along. Clearly, you are the expendable one. You are the one holding them back from accomplishing their mission and getting out of this mess alive. It is only a matter of time before somebody shoots you in the back or tosses you out of the airlock. In fact, maybe they plan on doing you in right now."  
**Special:** This obstacle is a single-hand Survival Test with no Rising Tension.

* **If the character passes the test,** they realize that their mind is playing tricks on them and there is no real need to worry.  
* **If the player fails this test,** their character freaks out and runs from the room, racing away from the rest of the crewmates. The paranoid player will stop running once they encounter another obstacle, which they must then test against, alone.  
* **If a player busts on this test,** they freak out and flee until they encounter another obstacle, as above. But their panic blinds them from noticing the hazard. They lose a Trait as damage and are then able to face off against the new obstacle as normal.

**Note:** Due to the personal mental nature of this test, it cannot be spit on doubles to allow another player to intervene.  
**Parasite / Illness (6♦, personal)**"You realize that for the last few hours your throat has been a little bit scratchy. At first you thought it might just an effect of the stress you are under, but now you are realizing it is something more serious. Your glands feel swollen, your joints are aching, and you are sweating more than you probably should be. The room starts spinning and you are struck with the sudden urge to throw up."  
This obstacle represents a deadly disease or unseen parasite that has somehow infected the targeted character.  
This might be something as simple as the common cold or as dangerous as rogue nanobots that have begun taking over the character's body for their own nefarious purposes.

* **If the player succeeds on this test,** then their character's immune system manages to eliminate the threat with no further ill effect, for now.  
* **If the player busts on this test,** their character has become visibly ill and loses a Trait as damage.

**Note:** This test cannot be split on doubles to allow another player to intervene.  
**Power Core (K♦)**This room provides the energy that makes the rest of the ship go. Unlike engineering, which is often a tangle of machines, the power core is often a relatively simple and straightforward place. It is also not a safe place to linger.  
While their predecessors used combustible fuel to provide propulsion, most large starships rely on fusion or ion reactors. These reactors collapse matter or antimatter to provide enormous amounts of energy.  
The fuel source itself is often in a liquid or crystalline form, which must be stored somewhere secure, and there is usually heavy shielding in place to prevent radiation from leaking out and contaminating the crew.  
Most power cores are connected to batteries which store up excess energy and slowly release it as needed. Depending on the needs of the ship, this may mean that the power core is not continually running, but only fires up at certain times to provide a surplus during combat or to increase speed.  
Conversely, powering down the core in combat or an emergency might be standard procedure to prevent a nuclear meltdown if the reactor sustains damage.  
Power cores are rarely small enough to fit in the space of single deck of the starship, and thus there may be exits from this room on multiple levels. Often, the core is accessed at various points by interconnected scaffolding and catwalks which make for excellent hiding places, escape routes, and ambush points.  
**Radiation (4♠, group, persistent)**"A pulsing light emanates from somewhere in this room, so bright that you can't quite make out where it is coming from. It hurts your eyes, forcing them closed, but that is not the real threat here. The light is just a vector for the silent killer that has already pierced your body and started ripping your DNA to shreds."  
In the movies, radiation has a much more visceral and immediate effect than in real life situations. In science fiction, radiation is often the cause of rapid mutations, instant bubbling burns, and even sudden death.  
Something in this room is emitting those extreme levels of cinematic-style radiation.

* **If the players succeed on this Survival Test,** they escape the room before gaining a critical dose of radiation.  
* **If a player busts on this test,** they must lose a Trait as damage but may progress forward to a new location.

**Note:** Any characters wearing a spacesuit are immune to the effects of this obstacle. They may attempt a Simple Test to shut down the radiation emissions to make the room safe for others.  
**Ready / War Room (J♠)**Part conference room, part officers lounge, and sometimes part tactical display, the ready room is a multifunctional space for meetings and planning sessions. It may also serve as a central command center in emergencies or for monitoring crews on offship missions.  
On a military vessel this room may be strictly designed for efficiency and function, but on other ships there is usually considerable thought given to the comfort and dignity of the space. In the later case, it may also be used for negotiations or meetings with visiting dignitaries.  
A large table dominates the center of this room, often covered with computer screens or holographic projectors and surrounded by high-backed chairs. There may also be models or maps prominently displayed on the walls. The lights can be dimmed, the windows can be darkened, and active security measures against spying or recording are almost always engaged.  
As this is a room that powerful people may have to spend considerable time in during a crisis, there is often a sideboard with coffee and snacks, or the equipment to produce those necessities on demand.  
**Rec Room (10♠)**The rec room is a place for off duty crew members to socialize, relax, and unwind. This room often contains simple, competitive games such as ping pong, billiards, and darts.  
Chairs and small tables are common furnishings for the rec room, as is at least one sofa or couch. Shelves containing board games, video games, and decks of cards are as likely as are machines for dispensing coffee, non-alcoholic drinks, and snacks.  
This room is usually well lit and comfortable in order to create the environment for a comfortable camaraderie, but there are not very many places to hide.  
**Repair Shop (2♣, special)**This workshop serves as a central office for the general maintenance staff of a starship and a place to fix computers, robots, and other small equipment.  
While the crew of engineering deals with the upkeep of the ship's engines, most other repairs such as maintaining the life support system, opening stuck doors, and replacing broken viewscreens fall to the people who share this space.  
The walls of the repair shop are lined with tool racks and workbenches covered by disassembled equipment and many spare parts. Amperage meters, computers, and diagnostic equipment are scattered about and easily accessible. Overhead lighting is supplemented by directional lamps, and the air is filled with the everpresent scents of oil and ozone.  
The floor here is most likely scuffed and stained and what little free space can be found on the walls is invariably filled with schematics and blueprints or advertisements for fancy tools and machine parts. In most ships there are multiple exits from this room, as the rec room is often centrally located and easily accessible.  
There are likely many things to hide behind and possibly even a few blunt instruments that could be used as a weapon, if the Dealer allows it.  
**Special:** Any characters with the Android Aptitude may attempt a Simple Test in this location after all obstacles have been defeated to recover a single lost Trait of damage. This is only allowed one time per Android character, no matter how many trips they make to a repair shop.  
**Robotics (8♦)**While damaged robots will most likely be found in the repair bays, functioning off-duty robots are kept in the robotics room. This is a place for charging and storing the shipboard robots that are not currently in use.  
The walls in this room are bare of any decoration, though they may feature status screens for the robots currently charging or numbers used to designate and organize where certain units should be kept.  
The floor is painted with various lines and symbols to facilitate traffic patterns at the start of a shift and to guide the less advanced models to their resting destination.  
There are most likely at least two exits from this room and at any given time a handful of robots will be found here.  
There is nothing useful here in the way of weaponry, but if a robot is charged up and simply awaiting orders, they could be put to use as a distraction for enemies.  
**Safety (A♥♦, special)Special:** Nothing bad happens in this room. Each PC in this room may now rest and recover an Exhausted (non-busted) Trait.

* **If a crewmate has died during the game,** the players must beat a Simple Test with one level of automatic Rising Tension to take advantage of the Safety.

**Science Lab (K♠♣, explosives, medkit)**Various laboratories are set up in starships to study many different things. On a military vessel, the lab might be for dealing with biological and chemical warfare or used to analyze unknown species or substances. In an exploration vessel, the lab might be tasked with running various experiments and making new discoveries. Regardless of the purpose, most laboratories will contain some of the same basic features.  
All science labs will be fitted with computers and workstations often with microscopes or scanners built in. There will be various containment measures from lockable cabinets to fire suppression and quarantine seals on the exit doors.  
If the lab is dealing with biohazards, there will most likely be a way to completely destroy the contents of the lab in a controlled fire or by venting them out into space.  
Labs are usually well lit and reconfigurable based on the needs of the ship and projects that may arise.  
**Medkit:** Due to the fact that accidents happen in labs, and a delay in first aid could be fatal, all labs contain ready-made first aid kits that are easily accessible.  
After defeating any obstacles in this room, one PC may take a single-use medkit from this room.  
**Explosives:** Most labs will contain chemical compounds that can be used to synthesize poisonous gasses or explosions. A Survival Test is required to find and mix these components safely.  
If facing an Adversary in this room, this test may be completed before the Adversary Survival Test is attempted. This represents an immediate use of explosives, which can negate the need for the following test.  
**Security Headquarters (3♣, weapons)**Part armory, part office, and part monitoring station, the security headquarters is responsible for the safety of the crew and passengers onboard. The personnel usually stationed here are the ones who investigate shipboard crimes, serve as guards, and chase down intruders who gain access to the ship.  
The most useful feature of this room is an array of video screens that show what is happening in other locations on the ship. The video feed is stored to computers in case it needs to be recalled and reviewed, but living personnel are also required to monitor the cameras in real time to identify anomalies and threats.  
There will be feeds kept of every public location, though only the most sensitive areas might be continually observed. There are usually no cameras in private quarters, the ready room, or in bathing or changing rooms.  
The security headquarters also contains controls to remotely lock down individual doors, entire rooms, or even whole sections of the ship and to activate and direct automated defense systems, such as laser turrets, lighting, and emergency fire suppression.  
Officers in the headquarters can make shipwide announcements or simply access speakers to communicate with a single room.  
**Weapons:** The security headquarters does contain a small, locked armory. After defeating any obstacles in this room, each character may acquire a ranged or melee weapon.  
**Security Malfunction (J♣, group)**"A quick scan of the room shows that it is well defended. There is a small security camera in the corner and a ceiling-mounted laser turret in the center of the space. A little white light blinks steadily on the side of the camera, and the lense zooms in as if evaluating whether or not you all are a threat. Moments later, it seems to have made its decision. The turret spins around towards you, small red targeting dots float across your bodies and then it opens fire."  
This room has an automated defense system that has targeted the characters as intruders. This might be because of faulty programing or the intervention of an evil AI, or it might simply be targeting anyone who moves through this space without giving the proper passcode.  
This obstacle is not simply an alarm, though that might happen as well. The defenses in this room should actively try to eliminate the perceived threat.

* **If the players succeed on the Survival Test,** they have dodged out of the way or managed to shut down the defense system.  
* **If a player busts on the test,** they have been hit and lose a Trait as damage.

**Storage (6♠, explosives, medkit)**Dedicated storage units on a large starship are essential to maximize the usable space in an efficient manner and to keep individual rooms workable and free of clutter. Even supplies used often will most likely be catalogued and kept in storage, as expendables need to be carefully rationed to last throughout the mission.  
Storage rooms usually have only one large door to enter or exit from. They are often situated against the hull of the ship to make use of oddly shaped spaces and to provide a buffer between the hull and populated areas.  
These rooms are minimally lit, often contain rows of stocked shelving or stacked crates, and are well organized. While it is doubtful that storage rooms provide secure long-term hiding places, there should be plenty of things to duck behind for cover, or to lie in wait for a pursuing foe.  
**Medkit:** After defeating any Obstacles in this room, one player may obtain a single-use medkit as gear.  
**Explosives:** Storage rooms are often stocked with ammunition or other materials that could be combined to make an explosive. A Survival Test is required to find and prepare these materials safely.  
If facing an Adversary in this room, this test may be completed before the Adversary Test is attempted. This represents an immediate use of the explosives which can negate the need for the following test.  
**Structural Issues (Q♠, group, persistent, special)**"One of the core assumptions about rooms in general, no matter where they are found, is that basic elements such as walls, ceilings, and floors tend to stay where they were originally placed. Unfortunately, that is not the case in this room. There is a loud, tortured groan that reverberates through the air, as if the starship itself was protesting against extreme abuses. Then the floor begins to buckle, the walls begin to bend, and the ceiling comes crashing in."  
Whether through external impact, neglected maintenance, or in the case of trash compactors, by the very design, the shape of this room is changing in a violent and dangerous manner.  
The characters either need to brace the collapsing room with something that will hold it stable long enough to pass through, or they must escape from the room in the mere seconds that remain before being crushed.

* **If the players succeed on this Survival Test,** they have managed to brace the room from collapse or have successfully dived back into the room that they came from.  
* **If a player busts on the test,** they have been partially crushed in their escape and lose a Trait as damage.

**Special:** If the characters manage to somehow shore up the room, there should remain a constant threat of collapse.  
Each round that the characters remain doing something else in the room, such as using an ability, taking supplies, or trying to repair a system, the Dealer should draw one card from the Room & Obstacle Deck.  
If the card is red, the room starts to collapse again and the characters within must try a new Survival Test to quickly escape. If the card is black, no test is needed for now.  
If the characters do not try and brace the room, they must exit through the nearest door, which is usually the one they entered through. After this room has collapsed, cross it off the map. It has become impassable.  
**Teleport (7♦, personal, special)**"It starts as a slight hum, as if your ears were ringing and then becomes a prickling on your skin. The room starts to shimmer and you can see faint outlines of other objects superimposed on the ones you know are already there. The hum grows louder and your entire body begins to tingle, as if every bit of you was vibrating with a new influx of energy on a cellular level. As your crewmates watch in horror, you begin to dematerialize and fade away."  
The character has been targeted by an errant teleporter beam or has slipped into a rift in the space-time continuum and is now being warped away to a new location on the starship.  
If this obstacle does not make sense with the technical levels of the setting, the Dealer is free to treat this as the Trapdoor obstacle instead.  
**Special:** This obstacle is a single-hand Survival Test with no Rising Tension.

* **If the character succeeds on the test,** they manage to shake off the effects and remain where they are.  
* **If they fail the test,** they are teleported to a random room on the ship and must face a new obstacle there on the following round. The Dealer turns the narration back to the players who did not get teleported, and draws a new Room and Obstacle card for the separated character on their next round.  
* **If the player busts on the test,** they lose a Trait as damage during the teleportation process but are still sent to a new room on the ship.

This test CAN be split on doubles as another character jumps into the beam and knocks the first one out of the way. The interrupting player takes the full effects of the test, however, leaving the original target unscathed.  
**Time Rift (10♦, personal, special)**"There is a queasy feeling in the pit of your stomach and a fuzziness at the edge of your vision. This is followed by a strong sense of déjà vu as you look around the room. You really feel like you have seen this all happen before. And then it clicks. This did just happen. You have slipped backwards a few minutes in time to the room you just left, and for whatever reason you now have to live through it again."  
**Special:** The unfortunate character who encounters this obstacle is suddenly looped backwards in time and space to a previous event.  
That character, and only that character, is sent back to the previous room and must now encounter all obstacles that were originally drawn for that room, even if they have already been defeated or originally effected someone else.  
Once the Survival Test for the past obstacle has been resolved, if the character survived they return to the present time in the exact same spot from which they departed it, though possibly worse for the wear. If they died facing the time-shifted obstacle, they do not return to the present.  
The other PCs are effectively paused while this event is resolved. They notice no passage of time from when the character jumps backwards to when they return, though they may notice if the person returns with new damage from the obstacle.  
If the character dies facing the obstacle again in the past, then everyone else remembers that they died and never entered this room in the first place.  
The Time Rift can create a situation where two characters are damaged or killed by the same obstacle in different timelines. One on the first encounter and a second who gets sent back to face it again.  
In this case, both realities remain. The memories of what happened to the surviving crewmates might be fuzzy, but they know that both characters were injured or killed in the normal flow of past events.  
**Torpedo Bay / Turret Room (10♦)**Usually found on military vessels, this room provides space to load torpedoes into their firing bays or to access the cramped gunner turrets that are situated on the outer hull of the ship. Even on exploration, transports, or rescue vessels some automated external defenses might be present and accessible as well.  
While these rooms don't usually provide the controls to fire the missiles or torpedoes, they do serve as ammunition storage and repair points. They will most likely contain emergency firefighting equipment and tools to help lift or transport the heavy ammunition.  
In the case of manually fired turrets, the controls and tactical displays are situated around a central gunner's seat.  
The ordinances in this room are too unwieldy or too specialized to be used by characters in combat, but a character might be able to hide successfully inside a firing tube or turret.  
**Toxic Gas (5♣, group)**"A soft hissing sound can be heard from somewhere in this room and within seconds of entering you start to choke and cough. Your skin itches and your eyeballs burn as you desperately gasp for breath, but your lungs fill up with poison instead. Your legs feel weak, your head is pounding, and you realize that if you don't get fresh air very soon then your next breath will most likely be your last."  
This obstacle isn't that the room lacks atmosphere, but that the air is filled with poison gas instead. This could be the result of spilled chemicals, from a malfunction in the life support systems, or from a trap intentionally set.  
If the characters do not describe some way of stopping the gas with their Survival Test, then they must flee the room the way they entered, seal the door if possible, and pick another route.

* **If the players succeed on the Survival Test,** they have managed to cover their mouth and escape the room without inhaling too much of the toxic gas.  
* **If a player busts on this test,** they have inhaled too much of the gas and lose a Trait as damage.

**Note:** Androids and characters wearing a spacesuit are immune to the effects of this obstacle and may be able to resolve the threat without a test.  
**Transporter (5♥, special)**The transporter room is capable of teleporting matter, both living and inert, to another location.  
If this room is unsuitable to the story being told, simply move one room up or down on the list or pick one that makes more sense.  
Transporter rooms are mostly open and empty, with nothing to use as a weapon and no place to hide. Their only furnishings consist of a scanning platform or chamber and a computer array to manage the complicated task of converting mass to energy, transmitting it to another location, and reassembling it once it has arrived.  
The scanners themselves might be able to handle multiple objects or beings at once, or they may be limited to a single transportable unit at a time.  
**Special:** The transporter can be used to send one or more crew members to a crucial location elsewhere on the ship.  
One PC must remain behind to operate the computer and must succeed on a Simple Test to accurately send the others to their desired destination.

* **If the test is successful,** the transported crew can travel to any single location they like. An obstacle will be drawn once they arrive.  
* **If the test fails,** the crew is transported to a random location on the ship. Draw two cards from the Room & Obstacle Deck and interpret them as normal.  
* **If the test is a bust,** the operator has made a significant and possibly life threatening mistake, but not for themselves.

Everyone in the transported team (but not the testing PC) is transported to a random room and loses a Trait as damage.  
**Trapdoor (J♦, personal, special)**"Perhaps it is the stress of the entire situation that causes you to overlook the warning signs or maybe the panel just blends in too well with the rest of the floor, but there is little warning before a trapdoor suddenly collapses beneath your weight and threatens to send you tumbling into the darkness below."  
**Special:** This obstacle is a single-round Survival Test with no Rising Tension.

* **If the targeted player succeeds,** they manage to jump out of the way of the trap door as it opens, or they grab on to the edge of the floor as they fall downwards.  
* **If this test is failed or busted,** the trapdoor opens below the character in question and drops them into a new location. Players that bust lose a Trait as damage from the fall.

After the character falls, the Dealer draws one card from the Room & Obstacle Deck to determine the resulting location.

* **If a red card is drawn,** the character is dropped into a waste treatment room and must face a new obstacle there on their next turn.  
* **If a black card is drawn,** the character is dropped into the maintenance tunnels. No new obstacle or Survival Test is required on their next turn, but they must follow the special rules regarding Maintenance Tunnels on page 78 should they wish to proceed through them.

No matter what, the character has dropped too far to simply climb back up and rejoin their teammates.  
**Vault (6♦, special)**The vault is a secure room on the ship for storing the most valuable cargo or supplies. There is rarely more than one exit from a vault and the door is impossible to open if the power is out.  
The vault contains whatever is being stored within its walls (perhaps in crates, on shelves, or in drawers) and often a sturdy central table. There is nothing extra in the vault to provide comfort or encourage lingering visits, and the room is almost always intently monitored by security cameras.  
There are usually defenses in place, such as a laser grid or valves for toxic gas, and the walls are extra thick and reinforced against impact damage or explosions.  
**Special:** Once all obstacles in it can be treated as a Safety to allow players to recover one Exhausted Trait. This is assuming the players have the ability to shut the door behind them and then reopen it when they want to escape. If that is not the case, then they cannot use this room to rest.  
**Vent to Space (4♣, group)**"Without warning a hatch opens up in the wall and suddenly the room is exposed to the cold vacuum of outer space. For whatever reason, the contents of this room are being jettisoned and you along with them\! You can't help thinking, as objects go flying past and you struggle to grab on to something secure, that there really should have been an alarm bell before all this happened."  
While this obstacle is just as dangerous, it differs from a Hull Breach in scope and persistence. The "vent" is a procedural action on the part of the ship that has either been deliberately triggered or is the byproduct of a system malfunction.  
It is unlikely that any characters will get sucked out into space, unless they choose that fate after the test has killed them, and there is no way to use this obstacle to initiate an EVA attempt.

* **If the players succeeded on their Survival Test,** they manage to hold on to something until the vent closes once again.  
* **If a player busts on the test,** they lose a Trait as damage. This could represent getting slammed into the wall or from being struck by another object being sucked towards the hole as well.

Once every character in the room has resolved the Group Test against this obstacle, the vent closes until this obstacle is drawn again.  
**Vermin Swarm (7♠, group, persistent, special)**"When you first enter this room, everything seems calm and normal. It may even be a safe place to catch your breath. Then you start to hear a very soft sound, some sort of crackling or chittering. It grows steadily louder and louder, until suddenly a wave of vermin bursts forth from the vents overhead and barrels into you. They crawl up your body; biting, scratching, and threatening to completely overwhelm you. Once done with you, they then move on to your crewmates."  
The vermin swarm can be anything that makes sense for the particular scenario; rats, spiders, cockroaches, nanobots, cuddly puffballs, stinging insects, or even alien bats; so long as they are small, act as a horde, and are incredibly difficult to eliminate.  
**Special:** The vermin swarm is a unique obstacle that acts like an Adversary, but must be tested against individually by each person who encounters it and not as a Group Test.  
No matter a player's result on the test, the swarm will sweep over their character (causing damage on a bust like normal) and on to the next player in the turn order.  
Once all characters in a room have resolved their test against the swarm, it will move on to the room from which the characters entered.  
If the players return to that room, they will have to all test against the swarm again before it moves on. The swarm always moves to a room the players have already discovered, never to the next room in their progression.  
If a player manages to eliminate the swarm on their Survival Test, perhaps by using a flamethrower or other methods of mass destruction, the swarm is fully destroyed and no one else needs to test against this swarm again.  
**Waste Processing (Q♦)**The classic example of a shipboard waste facility is the soggy, alien infested, trash compactor, though this room can also be represented by an incinerator, compost pile, refuse dump, or even a large sewage cistern. In almost every instance, this room is foul smelling and dirty.  
While a room for waste might actually contain an abundance of material, nothing found within is going to be particularly useful or even functional.  
Most often this room will be accessed from above, and it may prove difficult to climb back out, thus requiring a Simple Test to escape. If there is a door at ground level, it should be openable from the inside due to shipboard safety standards.  
Most obstacles drawn for this room should be useable with a little bit of creativity. Structural integrity could indicate crushing walls, vent to space could be an automatic jettison of waste, and Adversaries should almost always be an alien trash monster lurking in the muck.  
Fire, toxic gas, falling debris, and psychological assaults are also absolutely appropriate when one suddenly finds themselves lost amidst the garbage.  
**Weapons & Shields (J♥)**The Command Bridge often has dedicated officer stations for firing the weapon systems of a ship and monitoring the shields, but this room represents another tactical command space focused solely on that priority.  
The walls are lined with displays reporting on the status of the starship itself and that of enemy vessels, and there is a plethora of computers dedicated to calculating firing trajectories and modulating shield frequencies.  
No matter the vessel, this room has a decidedly military feel. There are swivel chairs at each computer, headsets for listening to updates and relaying commands, and toggle switches to activate the heavy weaponry. There may also be monitors that feed in video streams from the bridge, the hangar bar, and the torpedo tubes.  
The captain of the ship, or in some cases the chief defense officer aboard, has a central station in the midst of the chaos to watch over every other station and make informed combat decisions.  
There are no good hiding places in this room. There are also rarely any weapons available, so as to avoid a possible mutiny in the heat of battle.

### Chapter 5: Premade Scenarios

#### Flying into the Sun

**Major Crisis:** Collision Course (Ace)**Minor Crisis:** Lost (King)  
It is the year 3115\. You are the crew of the Starship Welke, the final hope of a dying species.  
Decades of war, plague, and famine have ravaged the Earth and left billions of people dead. Realizing that they must finally abandon their home, the wisest of humanity built giant starships and set course for the most likely habitable planet, Kepler 442b, over 1292 light years away. They named their destination Avalon.  
Seven ships were launched, with the passengers and crew placed in suspended animation once the vessels had cleared the asteroid belt. Their progress was to be monitored and maintained by the shipboard AI and semi-autonomous robot caretakers. The ships would be powered through solar energy by unfurling charging sails as they skimmed past the stars along the way.  
In an emergency, a small crew would be awoken from stasis to deal with the problem. Unfortunately, this crew would not be able to return to sleep, and would have to live out the remainder of their lives onboard the ship, never reaching their final destination. It was hoped that there would be no emergencies.  
As the haze clears from your eyes and your lungs gasp for breath in the stale recycled air, you realize that this hope was misplaced. The panel on your pod slides open and you can hear the distant blaring of alarm bells.  
"Jesus," someone mutters, "Can we shut that damn thing off?"  
Someone else barks out a demand, "Status report\!"  
A robotic voice fills the air. "Good morning, crew of the Starship Welke\! I hope you had a pleasant slumber. I regret to inform you that I have had to activate the emergency protocols. We are still 417 light years away from Avalon and have developed an error with our trajectory."  
It is surprisingly hot in this chamber and sweat beads up on your brow. Maybe the air conditioning just hasn't been turned on yet.  
"By my calculations," the computerized voice continues, "an asteroid storm caused a slight wobble some time ago. I must now inform you that given our current rate of travel, in six hours and fifty-three minutes the Starship Welke will crash into the nearby sun."  
This is the default scenario for Starship Infernum, and perhaps the most high stakes survival mission possible. No matter what they do, the crew is doomed, as even if they succeed on the mission they will still die of old age long before the Welke reaches her destination. But they may be able to save the ship and its passengers. In this way, they will be memorialized as heroes.  
PCs should be human characters from any background that they think would be useful in this situation. If they so desire, one PC could even play an android who has been awake the entire time or was only recently activated to assist the other crew members. The Aptitudes of Armored and Shapeshifter should not be used. The crew should also not begin the game with any equipment.  
The Welke itself is eerily empty and rather like a tomb, though there are scattered signs of previous emergency crews being woken up to deal with other crises. On the whole, living spaces in the ship, which were to be used as shelter once the Welke landed on Avalon, are still in their unopened state. The arrangement of rooms makes no logical sense, as each module was designed to be removed from the whole upon arrival. There are no maps to navigate the starship nor helpful information in the computers.  
Occasionally though, the crew may find entire rooms that were converted to a micro colony in which the other previous crews lived out their lives. Strangely enough, there is no evidence of any corpses.  
The lack of dead bodies ties directly to the Adversaries on the ship. In an effort to keep things clean and ready for the colonists once the ship finally arrives, the corpses of the previous crews have been reanimated as nanotech zombies. Thankfully there are not many of them, but they do shamble around the ship cleaning up messes and attacking things that are out of place, such as the current crew.  
Other options are to have aliens such as giant insects or greyskins lurking about the ship, or the AI could have gone insane and has spent thousands of years waking up crews and watching them die. This episode could also lead into future ones, if you so desire, by playing out the crew as long-term caretakers of the Welke having to deal with future catastrophes.

#### Prison Break

**Major Crisis:** Mutiny (Ace)**Minor Crisis:** Any except Missing Crewmate or Lost  
"Alright, listen up\! This is what we know about the current situation.  
Yesterday, at 0700 Solar Standard Time the transport freighter Pembroke 13 reported in as scheduled. Her cargo: 1,200 convicts bound for hard labor in the hydrogen mines on Jupiter. The report lists the ship's position, trajectory, fuel reserves, and duty rosters. It also notes the fact that three prisoners had been moved to solitary confinement for fighting.  
Yesterday, at 1900 hours the scheduled report was not received. The positional beacon onboard did register as fully functioning at that time and headed in the right direction.  
At 2235 a distress signal was received from the ship. It lasted nine and a half minutes before it was manually turned off. The ship's automated positional beacon was also deactivated shortly thereafter. Multiple attempts to contact the Pembroke 13 have been made by the Prison Authority.  
At 0100 this morning, the ship was located 27 clicks off course. We now believe it is headed for a pirate outpost on Saturn.  
Here is what we don't know. One, who is in charge of the ship right now. Two, what happened that caused the distress signal to be activated. Three, if any of the crew or prisoners are still alive.  
Your mission is to intercept the Pembroke 13\. Infiltrate the hull in a secure area. Analyze the situation, and if necessary, retake the ship. If this ship makes it to Saturn, we are gonna have an interplanetary incident on our hands and you kiss that Christmas bonus goodbye.  
So, suit up and move out. Let's, go people."  
This is a near future, rescue mission scenario. The PCs are a team of special forces astronauts tasked with infiltrating an un-known crisis. This scenario could also be used for the starting mission of an episodic campaign revolving around this team of interstellar problem solvers.  
The Pembroke 13 is a rust bucket transport ship that routinely makes the three month journey between Jupiter and Earth. It carries prisoners to the hydrogen mining operation on Jupiter and returns loaded down with canisters of the precious gas. Both the interior and exterior of the ship show significant wear and tear.  
This is not a vessel built for pleasure cruises; it is clunky, oft repaired, and utilitarian. It should have a very industrial feel with bare metal, open spaces, and almost zero amenities.  
The PCs in this scenario should all be near future humans that are part of a special strike force and are used to working together with military precision.  
The character Aptitudes of Militant, Sanitation, Shapeshifter, Smuggler, and Trainee should not be used for this scenario. All PCs begin with a weapon, explosive, or med-kit of their choice.  
The facts of the situation are that the prisoners have overthrown the guards and taken over the vessel. They are most likely holding the command crew as hostages. Adversary obstacles should only be the former prisoners (Pirates), mostly unarmed, unless the Minor Crisis of Rogue AI is also drawn. In that instance, maintenance droids could also pose a problem.  
As written, this is ultimately a non-fatal scenario. The PCs could decide to cut their losses and abandon the ship. If that happens, there should be narrated re-percussions or future problems if they are part of a longer campaign.  
If the PCs remain on the ship but do not manage to resolve the crisis before time runs out, they have been captured by the pirates and taken prisoner. Most likely they will kept alive and their escape could be the subject of future missions.

#### Scavengers

**Major Crisis:** Alien Horror (Ace)**Minor Crisis:** Missing Crewmate (5)  
Lost Grizzled Voice: "Space, it ain't a friendly place. All sorts of disasters happen all the time. You got wrecks of long forgotten battles. You got empty hulks from some explosion, maybe an accident, maybe on purpose. You got satellites that ran out of juice. All sorts of junk, just floating around and getting in the way.  
But that junk is mostly metal, with the occasional power cells still full or some electronic tech not too outdated. And that stuff is worth money for those brave enough and desperate enough to go after it. That's what we do. Salvage old wrecks, haul the scrap away and turn a profit. Kinda like deep sea divers. Only there's less sharks out here in space.  
It's a cutthroat business though. There aren't any salvage rights in the arse end of the galaxy. Whoever gets to the prize first, and gets out of there before anyone else shows up, is the victor. Those too slow? They usually wind up dead.  
Which is why I'm surprised nobody has gotten to this one before us. Huge friggin ship, some sort of alien tech, crashed into an asteroid and smashed almost in half. We've been working on it for a few days, gutting what we think is the server room. This stuff has to be worth some money.  
Of course we checked the engine core first. Don't know what it ran on, but there ain't any radiation down there and we can't find anything that even looks like fuel. This beast has probably been drifting here a long dang time. We brought down some lights, a generator, and some portable air scrubbers. With that and the little bit of false gravity the speed of this asteroid provides, we don't even need a spacesuit to walk around.  
Working on a ship like this can give you the willies, though. It is pretty dark, even with the generator lights. And it is lonely. We make better time splitting up and getting the small stuff on our own. Sometimes, when you are cutting away at a panel, you would swear you see something moving out of the corner of your eye.  
Sometimes, when you got a plate vibrating just right, the squealing metal starts to sound like distant screams. That's when you just turn up the tunes, block out your fears, and keep on working. Ain't like there is anything alive on this hunk of junk anyh-urk..."  
Second Voice: "Hey Stein, can you come here and give me a hand with this panel. It is stuck on something tight and I need to get to the wires behind it... Stein?... Hey Adlai, are you there man? Come on. Stop fooling around... ... ...  
Hey guys... Has anybody laid eyes on Stein lately? His coms seem to be down. Holy crap, guys\! What was that?"  
Set in the distant future where a plethora of junked space ships are everywhere and independent salvage workers find and strip them for scrap, this scenario is a classic setup for exploration and alien horror. To enhance the mood, you may want to experiment with dim or flickering lighting and creepy minimalistic music.  
The players are on an ancient, derelict vessel with no maps of the layout, no reliable power, and horrible things that are lying in wait to devour them.  
The ship itself is massive in scale. The control panels are clearly created for inhuman biology, the passageways are Gigeresque mechanical nightmares, and there are no doors to divide up com-partments of the ship or to provide a moment's respite from the chasing horror.  
PCs can be human or alien, if the Dealer allows, though the San-itation Aptitude should not be used in this scenario. It is easier to work without spacesuits and the crew has taken steps to at least make the air somewhat breathable.  
The only place on the alien ship that the crew can find weapons, explosives, medkits, or spacesuits is from their own small vessel in the makeshift hangar bay.  
The PCs should start the scenario separated and alone. They do have comms to communicate with each other, and should initially set out to find their missing teammate, as they cannot deal with the Alien Horror until he is found. When they do find Adlai Stein, or at least what is left of him, they are no longer sure of the way back to their own ship.  
The Adversary in this scenario should only be the Alien Horror. When drawn as a regular Obstacle, the PCs can fight it off or run from it, but they will not be able to kill it. The Adversary Level drawn indicates how many rounds it will chase them for before looping back and seeking easier prey instead.  
When a Joker is drawn and a disaster is triggered, the alien has ambushed the PCs and is in full on assault mode. Resolving a step of the Major Crisis either damages the alien, or helps set up a trap that will be able to defeat it in the end.  
If the PCs find their ship and attempt to escape, they will notice that the fuel lines have been ruptured and they must salvage parts to repair their own vessel from back inside the alien ship. Resolving a step in the Major Crisis on a doubles draw could then be seen as finding a part they need.

#### Space Madness

**Major Crisis:** Contaminated (Ace)**Minor Crisis:** Dignitary (Queen)  
"Captain's Log \- Space Force OneStardate: 7123947.7Location: Somewhere beyond the Zargon Nebula  
Horoscope for Today: Pisces \- Avoid the fish. It went bad three weeks ago. No amount of 'Cajun Spice' is going to make it taste any better.  
The condition on board Space Force One has grown dire. I have reason to believe that we have all now been infected with that nefarious aliment the medical team has dubbed 'Space Madness.' We cannot trust our own senses, nor our own judgment. To that end, I have transferred the burden of command to the coffee maker. So far, it is simply bubbling and gurgling. A most wise decision. Perhaps a hot drink will help us all.  
To make matters worse, Madame President is currently onboard. At least, I think she is. We cannot be entirely sure without a full cavity search. Why she decided on a surprise inspection all the way out here in the Zargon Nebula is beyond me. The last time we crossed paths, it was at the Grand Interstellar Parade marking the bicentennial anniversary of her reign. Truly, politicians are a strange beast and I do not understand her.  
While our medical staff has been busy euthanizing the sickest among us, the Science Squad has narrowed the source of the disease down to three possible vectors.  
One: Sabotage by my traitorous first officer. We all know that he has been eyeing my fancy chair of late.  
Two: That vial of green goop we scooped up for study on Syphilis Prime.  
Or Three: Last night's meatloaf.  
Symptoms of the disease include itching, sneezing, biting, spontaneous narcolepsy, willful disregard for one's own safety, a craving for fish, an overwhelming desire to shout one-liners, vertigo, and frequent public urination. Incubation time seems to be about fourteen seconds, and the contagion is spread through bodily fluids, dirty toilet seats, crisp high fives, awkward hugs, and prolonged eye contact. To minimize the risk of infection, before I put the toaster in charge, I temporarily outlawed eye contact and toilet use. I tried to forbid high fives and hugs, but HR would not let me.  
In a possibly unrelated event, that nice colony of leper nuns we picked up hitchhiking on the side of the hyper-route, after their shuttle bus broke down, has gone missing. To make matters worse, the crate of giant mutant banana slugs we were transporting to the preschool class on Skarlax Seven has been misplaced, and the doors to several of the containment cells on Security Deck Alpha were found open and empty this morning. Nobody remembers who or what was supposed to be in them.  
We cannot make port, we cannot rendezvous with a support vessel, and even I am not daft enough to send out a distress signal.  
If even one of the infected people onboard escaped the protective shell of Space Force One, this illness could infect all of humanity within hours. At this point, it seems we only have two options. Find a cure and inoculate everyone on board, burn the toilet seats, and sterilize all the surfaces. Or blow the ship up, and with it everyone on board; including Madame President.  
I am personally leaning towards the second option but we have to see what the refrigerator says. After all, we can always build another ship."  
This scenario is representative of a campy, tongue-in-cheek, classic 70's sci-fi episode. To maximize the session's enjoyment, the following tropes should be highly encouraged: cheesy overacting, stereotypical starship roles, tight color-coded uniforms, low-budget Adversaries, and smoldering looks directly at the camera.  
Space Force One is a sleek, high-tech behemoth of a ship with smooth lines, glowing interior surfaces, and spotlessly clean corridors. The lifts, doors, and computers operate mostly by voice command, though more complicated functions can be achieved through holographic interfaces.  
The PCs are all members of the Intergalactic Space Force. They can be human or alien, and can pick any Aptitude on the list.  
All PCs have access to public communication channels via microphones and speakers built into their insignia, and all weapons have been set to stun so that crew members do not accidentally kill each other. Explosives are not likely to be found on this ship.  
Adversaries should take the form of the rabid, leprous nuns (Bio-Drinkers), giant mutated banana slugs (Cuddly Breeders), a trio of escaped alien terrorist (Pirates), or crazed ship mates that have fallen to the final stages of Space Madness (Rogue Crewmates).  
The President may or may not be on the ship and thus solving the Dignitary Minor Crisis can either be someone actually locating her, or performing a scan of the ship and realizing that her life form is not among the inhabitants.  
The actual source of contagion could be any of the captain's proposed answers, or it could actually be coming from the leper nuns who are roaming the ship and eating the crewmates.

#### When You Wish Upon a (Dying) Star

**Major Crisis:** Dead Ship (Ace)**Minor Crisis:** Any except Lost  
In February of 1990, Polish Astronomer Aleksander Wolszczan discovered the pulsar star PSR B1257+12, otherwise known by the name of Lich. By 1994, it was discovered that three exoplanets orbited this star; Draugr, Poltergeist, and Phobetor. The search ensued for more planets around other neutron stars. Few were ever found.  
In October of 2204, a radio signal originating from the vicinity of B1257+12 was picked up by the Sirius Research Station. By December it had been identified as more than just an outburst of random electrical waves from the dead star over 2,300 light years away. It was an distress call, repeated over and over in all the languages of Earth. 'Help us. We are here.' It was the first and only message humanity had ever received from extraterrestrials.  
On August 1st, 2207 a colossal rescue ship was launched with a single mission: Investigate B1257+12 and confirm the existence, or remains, of extraterrestrial life and rescue them if need be. Using wormholes, the journey would take just over 3 years. The ship was named the Phoenix.  
The majority of the journey was uneventful. It wasn't until after the final jump, when the Phoenix finally entered the system around B1257, that the nightmares began.  
Horrible visions of tortured bodies on a cold, dead, landscape of ice; the ruins of strange architecture; and the giant bones of monstrous creatures plagued the unconscious mind. After the first duty rotation, nobody in the crew was able to sleep. They were all terrified of what the dreams might bring.  
As the Phoenix approached Phobetor, the radio signal, which had been constant up until then, suddenly changed to static and screaming. Scanning the planets for life produced strange, inconclusive results. There was something out there, but it wasn't on the surface of the planets. It was floating all around the Phoenix.  
Thirteen hours after arrival in the system, the power began to flicker and finally went out. Then the walls began to bleed. One by one, crewmates went missing or went mad and started hurting themselves.  
When strange creatures were reported in remote parts of the ship, the Captain and a small security team went to investigate. There were the sounds of gunshots. You have to assume that they're all dead. Just like the Phoenix. Totally dead, drifting in space, and assaulted by strange shadows. You are all that remains, a dwindling contingent of the original crew.  
It has been 72 hours and you are desperate at this point. If you work together, you might just be able to hold onto your sanity, repair this ship, and escape from this living hell.  
This scenario plays on the horror aspects of being lost in space and under assault by unknown forces. While this scenario could also work as a Dimensional Rift crisis, having the crew be stranded on a dead ship in a dead system adds a sense of isolation and desperation crucial to the story.  
The crew knows that they need to get out of here, but they simply are unable to do so. The longer they linger around this star, the worse everything will get.  
The Phoenix is a marvel of engineering, but in many ways it also feels like a Lovecraftian tomb. Built for the possible rescue of an untold number of survivors, it is cavernous and utilitarian, with exposed structures and criss-crossing support beams. The living spaces of the crew show clear signs of wear and tear after the three year journey and nothing is as shiny or new as when they started out.  
The crew should all be trained professionals from Earth and the Dealer should consider carefully before allowing the more exotic Aptitude abilities, such as Psychic, Shapeshifter, or Smuggler. No one may play the Captain, though other officers are allowed.  
Adversaries in this scenario should either be shadow beings (alter-dimensionals) or insane crewmates who have fallen into a cult like depravity (rogue crewmates). PCs should have access to walkie-talkies, weapons, and explosives.  
As the players deal with the crises, they should encounter strange sounds, such as screams suddenly cutting through their coms, blood on the floors and walls, shifting shadows, and lumps of meat that could be the remains of other crewmates. Outside of the ship, they can make out distant leviathans, colossal slow-moving entities with giant tentacles and too many eyes.  
If the crew escapes the Crises, there should be some lingering doubt about their fate. After they warp away, wrap up with everything going back to normal as they clean up the remnants and decide how to detail this in their reports.  
Then something scary happens to setup a sequel; the coms dissolve into static again, a single trickle of blood runs down the wall, or a shadow moves right before the credits start to roll.

#### Terror on Holodeck Three

**Major Crisis:** Dimensional Rift (Ace)**Minor Crisis:** Any  
You distinctly remember going to sleep in your usual quarters last night. You remember the crispness of the standard issues sheets, the soft glow from the blue illumination strip embedded in the wall, and the gentle, ever present hum of the warp engines as the Starship Redemption journeyed through space.  
It had been a long shift, sifting through the data collected from your explorations on Ribos Nine. There were many strange rocks to analyze and catalogue but thankfully no evidence of hostile lifeforms or dangerous bacteria. After a simple vitamin shake for dinner and a long, hot shower, one of the many perks of shipboard life, you practically crashed into bed and fell sound asleep within minutes.  
It takes several moments upon waking to realize that something is amiss. For starters, the air smells strongly of dust and mildew. Add to that the fact that the sheets are heavy, it is pitch black in your room, and the bed creaks as you shift. The sounds of the engines are missing and a startling silence has descended instead.  
You hear a sharp bang and someone outside your room starts to scream.  
You jump out of bed, your eyes struggling to adjust to the darkness, and rush to the door. It doesn't open automatically at your approach however, and you crash face first into a sturdy wooden surface.  
Dazed and rather confused, you glance down and see an antique glass doorknob, an artifact from a long forgotten age. You reach out hesitantly and turn it. The door opens up into a long hallway and standing in the middle of this hall is an unfamiliar lady screaming her head off.  
Gathered around the lady are several familiar faces, other members of your crew, though they aren't dressed in regulation uniforms. Instead, they are each attired in ancient Earthly fashions, like actors in a holovideo. You glance down at your own clothing and see that you are wearing pajamas from the same era.  
The hallway is not the familiar ship's corridor either. It is long, straight, and rectangular, not the gentle curving passage you are used to. The walls are lined with peeling patterned paper and the old wooden floor is rough beneath your bare feet.  
The screaming woman has fallen silent, and you can make out the body of a prone gentleman lying at her feet. There is a bleeding hole through his chest and a smoking pistol in her hand. As you rush forward, the lady, the gun, and the gentleman all dissolve into pixelated smears of color and then vanish completely. The puddle of blood remains.  
You are left standing there with your fellow crewmates wondering what just happened. Are you dreaming? If so, this is the most realistic dream you have ever experienced. Maybe the stress of the last few weeks has finally caught up to you. There isn't much time to ponder this thought though. The crack of another gunshot rings out in the distance again.  
The safety suppressors on Holodeck Three, which is usually used for training purposes or recreation, have gone haywire and trapped several of the crew members inside a simulation of an old hotel. Even though what they are experiencing is not real, it does affect all of the senses. It cannot, however, kill them.  
The crew must figure out the particulars of what is going on and find a way to reverse it before the rest of the crew realizes there is a malfunction and reboots the holodeck. If the PCs do not manage to escape before then, they will be trapped inside this false world forever.  
The rooms drawn from the Room & Obstacle Deck should be reinterpreted as their closest old world equivalent. If you have a copy of our haunted house horror game, Purgatory House, you may choose to use the room chart in that book instead. If you do this, the PCs can resolve steps of the Major Crisis at the Library, Boiler Room, and Workshop. All other rules, including the Crises and obstacles should still use the rules presented in this version of game.  
PCs can be human or alien, and any of the Aptitudes are useable. The Sanitation Aptitude can only be used after the first step of the Crisis is resolved and the players have discovered that they are inside a simulation. Then they may begin to equate the parallels between the rooms that they see and the actual locations on their ship. The characters should not begin with any weapons or gear and must discover these in play.  
There is no need to don spacesuits or do an EVA in this scenario, nor should the players be able to find their way into "outer space" in the simulation.  
Dealers are also encouraged to use an alteration of "clone" rules found on page 45 for this scenario. If a crewmate dies, they are respawned at their quarters in the simulation with one Trait eliminated already, but all others refreshed. They do retain their full memories of the events so far and do not trigger the need for a Simple Test on Safety rooms.  
The third death of a PC sees them waking up from the simulation with no Traits but keeping a single card from the test that eliminated them. They have not actually died, but have escaped and are able to act from outside the simulation with fresh insight on what is happening to those within. Instead of narrating the memory of a past event when they intervene, the player should narrate what they are doing in real time to aid those still stuck in the simulation.  
Adversaries can be anything the Dealer desires, and should be different each time a new one is encountered. The holodeck has a full library of assets to draw on, so feel free to have fun and mix things up. Likewise the rooms do not need to follow a common visual theme or make sense on how they connect to each other.  
There should be occasional glitches and clues that the world around the characters is not the real one, though simply ignoring most obstacles as illusion is not an acceptable solution.  
If the PCs manage to solve the crisis and escape, they return to reality. Give them a moment to celebrate and then wrap up the game with the realization that a real crisis, such as being attacked by another ship, is already in progress. They have jumped out of the frying pan and quite literally into the fire.
